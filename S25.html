From Frozen Harbin to Jade Jiuzhaigou — A Solo Winter Journey Across China

Paul Yeo  ·  5,000+ km across northern and southwestern China  ·  49 photos杨汉忠  ·  横跨中国北部与西地5000余公里  ·  49张照片

I went to China alone in winter — not because winter was easy, but because I wanted to see China without the crowds. From the frozen northeast to the jade lakes of Sichuan, this is what I found.

Stop 1 · Harbin 哈尔滨Frozen River, Burning Sky

Harbin is the kind of city that makes you realise how little you know about China. I had thought of China as south — warm, green, crowded. Harbin is the opposite. Northeast China, just a few hundred kilometres from Russia, with winters that drop to −30°C.

I arrived in the late afternoon and walked straight to the Songhua River. The river was beginning to freeze at the edges, long grey plates of ice pushing against the shore. The sky above was extraordinary — a wide amber and orange sunset with no buildings to interrupt it. Bare trees stood silhouetted against the glow. Cold enough that my breath clouded, warm enough in spirit that I didn't want to leave.

I stood there for a long time. No agenda. Just me and a river becoming ice.

Stop 2 · Beijing 北京Fog, Temples, and the Girl in Red

Beijing in winter is not what the tourist brochures show. The haze settles low over everything, giving the city a muted, impressionistic quality — lakes like pewter, temple walls fading into grey, the sun a red disc you can look at directly. I loved it.

I started at Shichahai, the old lake district in the Drum Tower neighbourhood. Early morning, almost no one around. A red pavilion stood beside the bare willows, the frozen lake behind it. I sat on a stone step and had a cup of tea from a stall. Simple and perfect.

The Summer Palace was busier but still quiet by peak-season standards. Walking through the arched moongates, each one framing the next, the mist turning the whole complex into something that felt ancient and slightly unreal. Near the lotus pond — brown stalks standing in black winter water — I came across something I will not forget.

A man lay face-down on a large blanket spread over the paving stones, painting a huge ink scroll on the ground. He had his ink stone, his bamboo roller, his reference papers. Completely absorbed. This was not a performance for tourists. This was just his morning. I stood and watched for five minutes, not wanting to disturb him.

Later, at a temple complex I found almost by accident, I watched a young woman in full red hanfu being photographed. She moved with the ease of someone who had done this many times — turning on the ancient walkway, holding up a Chinese opera lion mask to her face, red silk trailing behind her on the old bricks. The mist, the yellow-roofed towers, the flowing red fabric. I felt like I had stumbled into a classical painting.

"He lay face-down on the ground, painting a giant scroll beside the frozen lotus pond. Not for tourists. Not for anyone. Just for the art."

Stop 3 · Huanghua Cheng Great Wall 黄花城水长城Above the Reservoir

I had been to the Great Wall before — the busy sections at Badaling and Mutianyu. This time I went to Huanghua Cheng, a wilder, partially unrestored section north of Beijing where the Wall runs right above Miyun Reservoir. No cable cars. No souvenir stalls. Just stone and sky.

The climb was steep and the wind was cold. But when I reached the first watchtower and looked out, I understood immediately why I had come. The reservoir lay below — a long blue-grey strip of water between dramatic mountain ridges, framed by dark pine trees. I took photograph after photograph, knowing none of them would do it justice.

This section of the Wall has not been repaired in centuries. The steps are uneven, the battlements crumbling in places, the stone dark with weather. It was the Wall as it actually is — not a monument for a photo, but a thing that has been here through everything, slowly going back to the mountain.

Stop 4 · Shanhaiguan 山海关Where the Dragon Meets the Sea

Old Dragon's Head — 老龙头. This is where the Great Wall ends. Literally ends, stepping off the land and into the Bohai Sea. The Wall's final watchtower sits on the sand, waves lapping at its base, the horizon of the sea directly in front of it.

I arrived the evening before sunrise and stood at the wall as the sun went down. The sky turned extraordinary — mackerel clouds catching pink and orange light, the watchtower a dark silhouette against the sea. I could not believe this place existed, and that I was alone here.

At dawn I was back. I found an arched doorway in the wall that framed the sea perfectly — the dark stone arch, the grey water beyond, the first red light of sunrise on the horizon, two workers already on the platform doing maintenance. A perfect photograph that I did not plan.

Shanhaiguan itself is a pleasant city — old gates, fortress walls, a beach that stretches north and south. I walked the beach in the cold morning air, listening to waves, watching the container ships move slowly on the horizon. Alone. No schedule. Perfectly free.

Stop 5 · Tianjin 天津Canals, Quirk, and Pork Trotters

Tianjin sits an hour from Beijing by fast train and feels like a completely different city. It has a history of being a treaty port — French, Italian, British, German concession areas all side by side, a strange European architectural legacy in the middle of north China.

Walking the old streets I came across a building covered entirely in carved stone figures — rows and rows of Buddhas and deities in niches, all the way up several storeys, with the word "China" written in cursive script across the roofline. Eccentric, slightly surreal, uniquely Tianjin.

I found an old iron bridge over one of the canals and stood on it watching the water. Then, back in Beijing for my last night, I looked up the restaurant I had been told about: 猫嫂猪蹄 — Beijing pork trotters, slow-cooked until the meat falls away. I ordered a full serving, sat alone, and ate with great satisfaction.

The next morning I took a taxi to the airport. At the check-in counter I spotted the sign: 西雅航空 — Xiya Airlines. A small regional carrier I had never heard of. Flying west. Into Sichuan.

Stop 6 · Chengdu 成都Gateway to the Southwest

Chengdu feels warm even in winter. Not the temperature — November nights are cool — but the atmosphere. People sit outside at teahouses. Street food is everywhere. The city hums with a kind of contented busyness that is different from Beijing's ambition or Shanghai's pace.

I explored a riverside park — wide paths under tall trees, locals walking dogs, old men playing cards. A few shops nearby sold nothing but lottery tickets, their walls covered floor to ceiling with statistical charts tracking 大乐透 and 双色球 results. People studied these charts with the same seriousness a stockbroker studies charts. I bought one ticket. Did not win.

Chengdu was a day of rest before the real Sichuan began. I ate hotpot. I walked slowly. I prepared myself for what was coming: the Giant Buddha, the sacred mountain, and two places I had never even heard of a year ago — Huanglong and Jiuzhaigou.

Stop 7 · Leshan Giant Buddha 乐山大佛Face to Face with the Giant

The Leshan Giant Buddha is the largest stone Buddha in the world — 71 metres tall, carved into the red sandstone cliffs where three rivers meet. It was built in the Tang Dynasty, starting in 713 AD, and took 90 years to complete.

I knew all this. But nothing prepared me for actually standing there.

I walked the clifftop path and reached the point where you stand at the same height as the Buddha's head. The scale is simply not processable. His ear alone is 7 metres long. His shoulders are 28 metres wide. I looked at his face — the stone brow, the closed eyes, the full lips in a slight half-smile — and felt something I can only call awe.

From above, looking down, you see the full figure — head, shoulders, lap, feet planted on the riverbank. Crowds of tourists descend the narrow staircase beside him, tiny against his robe. He has sat like this for 1,300 years, watching the rivers, watching the dynasties rise and fall. He did not look impressed.

"His ear alone is seven metres long. I looked up at his face and felt something I can only call awe."

Stop 8 · Mount Emei 峨眉山Above the Clouds

Mount Emei is one of China's Four Sacred Buddhist Mountains. The lower slopes are lush even in winter — streams rush through gorges between moss-covered boulders, ferns hang over the path, bamboo groves open to misty lakes. I walked the lower trails slowly, in no hurry.

But the real reason to come to Emei in winter is the Golden Summit — 峨眉山金顶 — at 3,077 metres. In summer it is surrounded by cloud. In winter, if you are lucky, the clouds drop below you and you stand in clear air above them.

I took the cable car up and walked out onto the summit plateau. And there it was: the four-faced Puxian Bodhisattva on his white elephant throne, covered entirely in gold leaf, blazing in the winter sun against a deep blue sky. Ten storeys tall. Absolutely magnificent.

I walked around him slowly. Then I found the viewing platform and looked out. A vast sea of white cloud stretched in every direction, infinite and perfect. A single pine tree stood in silhouette against it, a distant temple peak just visible on a far ridge. I stood there for twenty minutes, not moving, not photographing. Just being there.

One of the great moments of my life. At 70, standing above the clouds in China.

Stop 9 · Huanglong 黄龙The Valley of Frozen Gold

Huanglong is not on the standard tourist trail. It is deep in northern Sichuan, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, famous for its travertine terraced pools — naturally formed limestone terraces, like the ones at Pamukkale in Turkey, but smaller, wilder, and at high altitude.

In summer the pools are turquoise blue. In winter, they freeze.

I arrived to find a landscape that looked alien. Long cascading terraces of cream-coloured limestone, frozen solid, some still holding shallow water in shades of pale jade. Above them, a Tibetan monastery in yellow and gold. And above that, snow mountains. The air was sharp and clean, the altitude noticeable.

I walked the boardwalk slowly, photographing at every turn. The ice had built strange shapes — mineral deposits visible beneath the surface, crystalline edges, a lone tree frozen in place. At dusk the light turned gold and the whole valley glowed. I did not want to leave.

Stop 10 · Jiuzhaigou 九寨沟The Last Paradise

I had heard about Jiuzhaigou for years. Everyone says the photos don't do it justice. They are right.

Jiuzhaigou — Nine Village Valley — is a high mountain valley in northern Sichuan with a chain of multi-coloured lakes fed by snowmelt. The water colour comes from the dissolved minerals and the lake beds of white calcium carbonate. In summer the lakes are vivid jade and emerald. In winter, some freeze, some stay open, and the water is the clearest I have ever seen.

I spent an entire day walking the valley. The lakes are at different levels, connected by waterfalls that still flow even in winter. I photographed the same turquoise lake from six different angles and could not choose between them. At one lake I could see fallen tree trunks perfectly preserved ten metres below the surface, the water above them perfectly clear. At another, I simply sat and stared.

The shuttle bus back in the evening was packed — a young person in full cosplay stood in the aisle in a white wig and bamboo hat, swaying with the curves of the mountain road. China's young people are extraordinary. Playful, creative, completely at ease with who they are.

On the last afternoon I stood at the edge of one lake and photographed the water one final time. Azure and teal, the rocky bottom clearly visible, snow mountains reflected in the surface. My favourite photograph of the whole trip. My favourite place in the whole of China.

"At one lake I could see fallen trunks ten metres below the surface. The water above them was perfectly clear. I stood there a long time, just grateful to be here."

A Note on Solo Winter Travel

People ask if it is lonely, travelling alone. Honestly? Not for me. I chose each day's agenda. I changed plans freely. I ate what I wanted, at whatever time I felt like eating. I stood at the Old Dragon's Head at sunset for an hour and there was no one to hurry me along.

China in winter has fewer tourists, kinder prices, clearer mountain views, and a different quality of light. The cold is manageable — dress for it. The quietness is a gift.

I flew home with 49 photographs and two weeks of memories that will stay with me for the rest of my life. From frozen Harbin to jade Jiuzhaigou. From the wall that ended at the sea to the lake that reflected the sky. China is too big to understand. But this trip made me love it more.

The most vivid water I have ever seen. Azure and turquoise and teal, the rocky lake bed clearly visible through water so clear it seems to have no colour of its own. Snow mountains above, bare trees at the edges. My last photograph at Jiuzhaigou, and my favourite of the entire trip. I took it and then put my phone in my pocket. Some moments are better finished than continued.
Photo 49 of 49 · Jiuzhaigou — Shuttle Bus
The scenic area shuttle bus back at 17:35 — packed with visitors heading out for the day, a young person in full cosplay standing in the aisle in a white wig and large bamboo hat, hanging from the overhead rail, swaying with the mountain road curves. This is modern China too. Young, creative, playful, completely at ease with who they are. I stood in that bus grinning. What a trip.
