The Secret BKE Trail
My Solo Hike to Upper Peirce Reservoir

By Paul Yeo  ·  18 June 2026  ·  Singapore
📍  See the Map 📖  Read the Story

Most people drive past the Bukit Timah Expressway without a second thought. I decided to walk it — and what I found on the other side changed my morning completely.

Why the BKE?

I chose this route for one simple reason: nobody hikes from the BKE side. The regular trails at Zhenghua Park and Dairy Farm are fine — but I wanted peace. Real peace. No joggers, no families with strollers, no cyclists calling out "on your left." I wanted the whole forest to myself.

So on the morning of 18 June 2026, I set off at 8 AM in my long black pants and long black t-shirt — my standard hiking uniform — with just one orange in my pocket. No energy bars, no hiking poles. Just me and my phone.

Into Zhenghua Park

I started at the open field near my area — tall blocks in the distance, bright morning sky overhead. At the old mossy Zhenghua Park signboard, I turned right into the Belukar Trail. The transition from concrete to jungle took about thirty seconds. One moment, HDB towers. The next, nothing but trees, roots and the sound of birds.

The Cat and Mouse on the BKE

Walking the BKE shoulder is not a normal thing to do. It is a highway. Pedestrians are not supposed to be here. But there was a narrow grass verge, and I had a plan: walk along it, find the forest entry, disappear into the trees.

What I did not plan for was the road recovery car.

The first time the worker spotted me, he leaned out and waved me back firmly. I stopped, looked around innocently — and the moment his truck went around the bend, I kept going.

"The second time he saw me, he stopped and reached for his phone. I didn't wait to find out who he was calling. I turned and ran."

Pure comedy: a 70-year-old retired man jogging along a Singapore expressway shoulder, diving behind a bush to escape a road recovery worker. I found my entry point: a gap beside a concrete pipe stone marked MB W039. I slipped through, pushed past the undergrowth — and I was in. Safe. Hidden.

And yes — I immediately used what is surely Singapore's best open-air restroom, completely invisible from the road.

The Forest Belongs to Me

Inside, it was a different world. No traffic noise. No people. Just tall trees, leafy shadows, and soft earth underfoot. White paint marks on tree trunks guided me — the silent language of forest hikers, meaning safe to follow. I passed ancient trees with enormous gnarled roots, and a rusting sign half-swallowed by vines: WARNING — High Pressure Pipeline in the Vicinity.

Beneath this quiet jungle, the city hums on, unseen.

Then, through the trees ahead — light. A bright opening. The reservoir.

Upper Peirce Reservoir — The Reward

I arrived at Upper Peirce Reservoir at around 9:10 AM. The water was glassy and perfectly still, the surrounding jungle reflected in it so clearly you could almost believe the world had been doubled. Clouds drifted above — and the same clouds drifted below, on the water's surface.

Nobody else was here. Not a single soul.

I found a spot on the bank and sat down. I peeled my orange. A cool wind came off the water — gentle, unexpected — and the surface broke into soft ripples. I sat there for a long time, just breathing.

"I thought: if my house had this view, I would never leave. Morning coffee here, every single day, for the rest of my life."

Nature came to join me. A butterfly — dark wings with vivid teal-green stripes — landed on the bark beside me and stayed a while. Then a scarlet dragonfly settled at the water's edge, wings catching the light like stained glass. I also spotted a cluster of vivid pink Golden Apple Snail eggs on a branch above the waterline, and further along, a tree covered in dark growths — sick, fighting something, but still standing tall.

Lost — No Signal, Low Battery

Around 10:51 AM I began making my way out. But the jungle does not give up its secrets easily.

My phone signal disappeared. My battery was critically low. The path I expected was not where I thought it was. I was surrounded by trees in every direction, and for the first time that morning, I felt something I had not felt all day: uncertainty.

⚠ Lost in the Jungle

No signal. Low battery. Alone in the forest near Upper Peirce Reservoir. I sent one message to my AI David — "Seek help" — and received directions before the signal died completely.

David's instructions were simple and clear:

"CALL 999 NOW before the battery dies. If no signal — listen for traffic. The BKE is to the WEST. Walk toward the sound of the cars. Keep the reservoir behind you and follow the lightest patches between the trees. Do not walk deeper. Stay near the reservoir edge where rescuers can find you."

I did exactly that. I stopped. I listened. Through the trees, faintly — the sound of traffic. I turned toward it. I walked slowly, watching for the white tree markers, watching for the light. Step by step, the sound grew louder. The trees thinned. And then — the path. The road. Sunlight.

By 11 AM, I was out. Safe. Back in the world of roads and signals and other people.

What Retirement Means to Me

People ask me what I do in retirement. Some expect me to say I sleep in, or travel, or spend time with grandchildren. Here is the honest answer: retirement, for me, means I no longer work — but I replaced work with something better.

My mornings belong to hiking. Either up to the summit at Dairy Farm, or down to the reservoir — like today. Every morning, I put on my long pants and long t-shirt and I go. Rain or shine, weekday or weekend. The trail does not care what day it is.

After the hike I go for a swim. Then lunch. Then I sit down and watch YouTube for an hour — whatever catches my interest that day, AI videos, travel, nature. Then I take a two-hour nap. Then the afternoon begins.

That is my day. That is retirement done right.

No alarm clock. No meetings. No boss. No performance review. Just the morning trail, the water, the food, the screen, the sleep — and then the rest of the day is open, waiting to be filled with whatever I feel like.

"Retirement is not the end of work — it is the beginning of a life where every morning is yours to spend exactly as you choose."

What This Hike Taught Me

I am 70 years old, retired, and I spent a Tuesday morning playing cat and mouse with a highway recovery worker, slipping through a gap in a fence marked No Entry, eating an orange alone at Singapore's most beautiful reservoir, getting briefly lost with a dying phone in a jungle — and finding my way out by listening to the sound of the BKE.

9,000 steps. Three hours. One orange. Zero regrets.

The BKE entry is not for everyone. You need long pants (the undergrowth is thick), good shoes, and a basic sense of direction. You need to know about white trail markers. You need to be comfortable with no path and no signage. And I would strongly recommend: tell someone where you are going.

But if you are willing to do all that — what you get in return is something the crowded, signposted, Instagram-famous trails cannot give you: a whole forest, and a whole reservoir, entirely to yourself.

"This is what retirement looks like for me — not a beach chair and a drink, but a secret trail, muddy shoes, and the whole of Upper Peirce Reservoir at 9 AM with nobody else in sight."
