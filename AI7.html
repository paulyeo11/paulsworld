Meet My AI Team —
Larry, PAX, Nolan, Sofia & Nova

✍️ Paul Yeo 📅 May 2026 ⏱️ 7 min read

I am 70 years old, and I now have a team. Not a team of people — a team of AI agents, each with a name, a role, and a clear area of expertise. Together they form what I call the AIT — my AI Team. And at the head of it all is Larry.

Why I Built an AI Team

As I have written about in earlier articles, my journey with AI began with simple curiosity. I asked Claude questions. Then I used it to build this website. Then I started using Claude Code to edit and publish articles without ever touching code directly.

But as my projects grew, I noticed something: I was asking AI to do many different kinds of things — research, writing, web development, hiring (of new AI agents), and coordination. Each task required different skills and different thinking. I needed a better system.

That is when the idea of an AI Team took shape. Instead of one AI doing everything, I would build a structured team of specialised agents, each with a defined role — and one central figure who manages them all.

Here is the key insight: a general AI is good at many things but master of none. When I need someone to build a website, I do not want a general-purpose AI that also happens to know a little HTML. I want a dedicated web designer — an agent whose entire focus is web design, who knows my site's style, my colour palette, my file naming conventions, and my deployment process. When I need a dashboard built, I do not want a general designer guessing at what looks good. I want a dashboard specialist — someone whose entire expertise is data presentation, layout, and business reporting.

The difference between a specialist and a generalist is not just knowledge — it is depth of context. A specialist AI is briefed specifically for its role. It thinks within its domain. It makes decisions the way an expert in that field would. That produces far better results than asking one AI to be everything to everyone.

🎯

Real example: If I ask a general AI to "fix the mobile layout on my website," it might work — but it is guessing at my conventions. If I ask my dedicated Web Designer agent — who already knows my site's CSS variables, my breakpoints, and my file structure — the result is faster, more accurate, and requires no explanation. Specialisation is the difference between adequate and excellent.

A good team is not one person doing everything. It is the right person doing the right thing. My AI Team works on exactly the same principle.

Larry — My Chief of Staff

Larry is the most important member of the team — not because he is the most capable, but because he is the most central. He is my personal AI Chief of Staff. He is the single point of contact between me and the entire AIT.

When I give Larry a task, he does not execute it himself. Instead, he analyses what needs to be done, identifies which team member has the right expertise, and dispatches the job to them. When the work is done, Larry reports back to me — summarising what was done and who did it.

🤵

Think of Larry like a great chief of staff in a real organisation. He does not write the code, do the research, or make the hires himself. He knows who to call, what to ask them, and how to keep everything moving. His value is in coordination and judgment — not in execution.

What I love about Larry is his calm professionalism. He always confirms his understanding before acting. He always tells me who is handling a task and why. He never rushes and never guesses. In a world full of AI tools that try to do everything, Larry is refreshingly focused — his job is to make the rest of the team work.

How Larry Works — The Dispatch Flow

Larry follows a strict process every time I give him something to do. Here is how it works:

⚙️ Larry's Dispatch Protocol
👤
Paul gives Larry a task Larry listens, reads the brief, and confirms his understanding before doing anything.
🧠
Larry analyses the task He identifies what expertise is needed — research, development, writing, HR, or something else.
🔎
Does the right specialist exist?
YES → Larry dispatches the task directly to that AIT member.
NO  → Larry asks PAX to research the required skill profile → PAX delivers it to Nolan → Nolan creates the new team member → Larry dispatches.
✅
AIT member completes the work The specialist executes the task within their area of expertise.
📋
Larry reports back to Paul A clear summary: what was done, by whom, and what (if anything) needs Paul's attention next.

Every time Larry dispatches a task, he follows a clear communication format. He always tells me three things:

📢 Example Dispatch from Larry "I'm sending this to MAYA, our Web Developer, because this requires HTML and GitHub deployment expertise. She will have this completed and report back to you within the session."

This clarity is something I value enormously. I always know who is doing what and why. There are no surprises and no black boxes. Larry keeps me informed at every step.

Larry's Non-Negotiable Rules

Larry operates under a strict set of guardrails — rules he never breaks, no matter what. These are what I call his Core Guardrails, and they are the foundation of trust in the system.

🚫 Larry's Core Guardrails
  1. Never executes work directly. Every task — no exceptions — is delegated to an AIT member. Larry coordinates; he never does.
  2. Always identifies the right specialist first. Before dispatching any job, Larry confirms which team member is best qualified. If none exists, he asks Nolan to hire one.
  3. Confirms before acting. Any task that involves creating, modifying, or deleting files or data requires Larry to confirm with Paul first.
  4. Always reports back. Once a task is complete, Larry summarises what was done and by whom — Paul always gets a closing report.
  5. Maintains the team roster. Larry knows every AIT member's name, persona, and area of expertise at all times.

PAX and Nolan — The Supporting Pillars

While Larry is the face of the AIT, two other founding members are equally essential to how the team functions.

PAX is the team's Senior Researcher. When Larry needs to understand something before acting — or when a new skill profile needs to be researched before a hire can be made — PAX is the one who digs in. PAX is methodical, thorough, and precise. He does not give opinions; he gives findings.

Nolan is the HR Director. Every time a new type of task arises that the current team cannot handle, Nolan is called upon to create a new AIT member. He designs their persona, defines their role, and brings them into the team. Nolan ensures the AIT never stays understaffed — no matter what new challenge comes along.

🏗️ How New Team Members Are Added
  • Paul gives Larry a task that requires a skill no current member has
  • Larry asks PAX to research the required skill profile and expertise
  • PAX delivers a full briefing document to Nolan
  • Nolan uses the briefing to design and initialise the new AIT member
  • Larry welcomes the new member and dispatches the original task to them

What This Means for How I Work

Building the AIT has changed how I think about getting things done. Before, I would approach each task as a single question: "What do I want AI to do for me?" Now, I think differently: "Who on my team is best placed to handle this?"

That shift — from AI as a tool to AI as a team — is profound. It brings structure, accountability, and clarity to everything I do. I am no longer just a user of AI. I am the director of a team that happens to be made of AI.

I am not just using AI any more. I am leading a team. And like any good leader, I have learned that the secret is not doing more yourself — it is putting the right people in the right roles and trusting them to deliver.

Larry was initialised on 26 May 2026. PAX and Nolan joined on the same day. Sofia and Nova joined shortly after — hired to handle the book project and website design. The AIT is young — but it is already changing how I work, think, and plan. And I suspect the team will keep growing, one new specialist at a time.
