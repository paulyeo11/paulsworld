One Month in Japan
Our Kyushu Road Trip

By Paul & Joanne Yeo  ·  October – November 2025  ·  Kyushu, Japan杨汉忠与乔安妮  ·  2025年10月至11月  ·  日本九州
📍  See the Route查看路线 📖  Read the Story阅读故事
📍 Our Kyushu Route — One Month, One Loop我们的九州路线 — 一个月，一个大环

One complete loop around Kyushu island. We drove every section of this route over one month — October to November 2025. Tap any marker to see the stop.一次绕行九州岛的完整大环。我们用一个月时间驾车走完了路线上的每一段 — 2025年10月至11月。点击任意标记可查看停靠点。

Japan had been on our list for years. Not just a quick visit — a proper, slow, deep journey. In October 2025, we finally did it: one month, a rental car, and the whole of Kyushu to explore at our own pace.

Why Japan? Why Now?为什么是日本？为什么是现在？

Retirement gives you something most people never have: time. Real time. Not just a week squeezed between meetings, but the ability to say — let's go for a month. Let's drive slowly and stop when we feel like it. Let's eat without checking the clock. That is how this trip was born.

We chose Japan because it combines everything we love: incredible food, beautiful nature, excellent hiking, and a culture unlike anything else in the world. And we chose Kyushu — Japan's southernmost main island — because it is less crowded than Tokyo or Osaka, yet every bit as extraordinary.

Kyoto First — Meeting Naca先去京都 — 与Naca重逢

Before the road trip began, we made a detour to Kyoto to visit our friend Naca. It was the perfect way to ease into Japan — old temples, narrow lanes paved with stone, the smell of incense drifting from shrines tucked between modern buildings. Kyoto does not feel like it belongs to the present. It belongs to a Japan from centuries ago, somehow still perfectly intact.

Naca showed us the Kyoto that tourists miss. Hidden spots. A little restaurant down an alley that only locals know. An evening walk along the Kamo River with the lanterns reflecting on the water. We sat and talked for hours — the kind of unhurried conversation you can only have when there is nothing else you need to do that day.

Kyoto reminded me that the best travel is not about ticking sights off a list. It is about being somewhere fully, with people who matter.

"Kyoto reminded me that the best travel is not about ticking off sights — it is about being somewhere fully, with no rush and nowhere else to be."

The Drive Begins — Keys in Fukuoka旅程启动 — 在福冈拿到钥匙

From Kyoto, we took the Shinkansen down to Fukuoka — Japan's gateway to Kyushu. The bullet train still amazes me every time: silent, smooth, impossibly punctual. We arrived exactly on schedule, picked up our rental car, and set the GPS to "no fixed plan."

Driving in Japan is a genuine pleasure. The roads are immaculate. Drivers are patient and courteous. Signage is clear. Petrol stations have staff who wipe your windscreen without being asked. Everything about the driving experience is thoughtful and calm — very different from Singapore's highways.

We headed west first, toward the coast. The adventure had begun.

Nagasaki — History Held Gently长崎 — 以温柔承载历史

Our first major stop was Nagasaki. This city carries enormous history and enormous pain, yet it does not drown in either. The Peace Park and the Atomic Bomb Museum are places you need to visit slowly, quietly, with no phone in your hand. Joanne and I walked through them without speaking much. Some places do not need narration.

But Nagasaki is also a city of colour and life. The Dutch colonial influence is everywhere — in the architecture, the food, the street names. We ate Champon, the thick noodle soup unique to Nagasaki, in a small restaurant run by an elderly couple who did not speak a word of English. We ordered by pointing. The soup was outstanding.

Kumamoto, Aso and the Hikes熊本、阿苏与山间徒步

Inland, the landscape shifts dramatically. Kumamoto Castle — largely rebuilt after a devastating earthquake in 2016 — rose above the city like a statement of Japanese resilience. From there, we drove toward Mount Aso, the largest active volcanic caldera in the world. You drive into it. The road winds down into the crater floor while steam rises from the ground around you. It is surreal, beautiful, and slightly terrifying in the best way.

We hiked a lot. Every region of Kyushu had its own trails — some easy, some very steep, all rewarding. I would wake early, while Joanne was still asleep, and go out into the quiet morning air. Just me, the path, and whatever was waiting at the top. My favourite kind of morning.

"I would wake before sunrise, lace up my shoes, and follow whatever trail was nearest. Every summit in Kyushu had its own reward. Japan's mountains do not disappoint."

The Food — Every Meal, an Event美食 — 每一餐都是一件大事

Japan feeds you like nowhere else on earth. Not just good food — perfect food. Even convenience store onigiri at 7-Eleven is better than most restaurant meals back home. The Japanese treat eating as seriously as any other craft.

We ate ramen in Fukuoka at midnight — the real tonkotsu, cloudy pork broth, thin noodles, melting chashu pork. We ate fresh sashimi in a port town where the fish came off the boat that morning. We found a tiny yakiniku restaurant where we grilled our own Wagyu at the table, and I understood, for the first time, what all the fuss is about.

We never ate badly. Not once in a month. That is Japan's quiet miracle.

The Honest Japanese Driver诚实的日本司机

Somewhere during the trip — I think it was in Miyazaki — we came back to our parked rental car to find a dent in the front bumper. My heart sank. Rental car damage, foreign country, how do we handle this?

But then I noticed something tucked under the windscreen wiper: a handwritten note. In Japanese, with a phone number.

The driver who had bumped us did not run away. They waited, left their details, and trusted us to call them. We later learned from our guesthouse host that this is simply what Japanese people do. It is the norm, not the exception. Honesty is not a special virtue in Japan — it is the baseline.

The rental company handled everything. Nobody argued. Nobody was rude. It was resolved so smoothly that it almost felt routine. I thought about this for days afterward.

🌎 A Japanese Lesson in Honesty

A Japanese driver bumped our rental car while we were away — and left a handwritten note with their contact details under the wiper. They did not run. They waited, then left. In Japan, honesty is not remarkable. It is expected.

"They bumped our car — and left a note with their number. They did not run. That small act of honesty said more about Japan than any guidebook ever could."

Beppu, Yufuin and the South别府、由布院与南部之旅

In Beppu we spent a full day at the famous "Jigoku" — the hell springs. Bubbling, steaming, vivid turquoise and blood-red pools of boiling water scattered across the city. It sounds ominous. It looks like another planet. After Beppu, we drove inland to Yufuin, one of Japan's most charming onsen towns. Quiet streets, craft shops, steam rising from every building. We checked into a ryokan and spent the evening soaking in the hot spring bath, watching the steam curl up into the cold night air.

Heading south, we drove through Miyazaki prefecture along the coast — dramatic cliffs, dark volcanic sand beaches, subtropical palms. Then to Kagoshima, Japan's southernmost major city, with Sakurajima volcano sitting across the bay, puffing smoke into the sky as casually as a kettle.

What One Month Taught Me一个月教会我的事

A week in Japan shows you the surface. Beautiful, yes — but surface. A month lets you go deeper. You start to notice the rhythm. The morning quiet before the shops open. The way people queue without being asked to. The care taken with packaging, presentation, service — not to impress, but because that is simply how things are done.

Japan is not a perfect country. But it is a country that takes its values seriously. Honesty. Craft. Respect. Order. These are not slogans — they are visible, every day, in everything from the way a ramen bowl is presented to the way a stranger waits by a dented car.

Joanne and I drove over 3,000 kilometres around Kyushu. We hiked mountains, soaked in hot springs, ate food that made us close our eyes with pleasure, and watched a volcano smoke from across a bay at sunset. We came home different — a little slower, a little more thoughtful, a little more careful about small things.

Japan does that to you. And I cannot wait to go back.

"Japan is not just a place you visit. It is a place that quietly teaches you something — about craft, about honesty, about how much better things can be when people simply try."
