The Walk Out the Door

Five Minutes from Home, a World Away

16 June 2026  ·  8:00 AM  ·  Dairy Farm Residences → Chestnut Nature Park

I stepped out of Dairy Farm Residences at eight o'clock in the morning. The air was still cool, the light still gentle, and the day had not yet made up its mind about what it wanted to be. Five minutes of walking, and I was standing at the entrance to Chestnut Nature Park, looking at the trail map bolted to a wooden board. You Are Here — a little red marker, right in the middle of all that green.

I have lived close to this park for a while now, and I had never done the Southern Hiking Loop. That is the thing about having a park practically on your doorstep: you tell yourself you will get to it, and then you keep getting to other things instead. Today, I finally got to it. Today I said yes.

"Eight o'clock in the morning, five minutes from my front door, and a trail I had never walked before. Retirement has taught me that the best adventures are often hiding closest to home."

I was alone. No company, no earphones, no agenda except to walk the loop and see what it showed me. That is enough of a plan. At seventy, I have learned not to overcomplicate things that are already good.

Into the Southern Loop

A Jungle Trail That Earns Its Keep

Chestnut Nature Park  ·  Southern Hiking Loop  ·  Singapore

The Southern Hiking Loop at Chestnut Nature Park is not a stroll. It winds, it climbs, it dips, it turns back on itself through jungle that feels properly wild — not manicured, not tamed, not designed for Instagram. Real jungle, with roots across the path and branches overhead and the kind of damp, rich smell that tells you the earth is alive and working hard.

The trail is well-marked but not crowded. On a Monday morning at eight, I had long stretches entirely to myself. Just me and the birds — and there were many birds, doing the things birds do in a Singapore forest at that hour: calling, answering, moving through the canopy in ones and twos, completely unbothered by the retired man walking below them.

I took it at my own pace. That is the privilege of going solo — no one to slow down for, no one to keep up with. Just the trail and me. I paused when I wanted to. I looked up when something moved in the canopy. I stood still when the forest went quiet for a moment, just to hear what quiet sounds like when it is full of living things.

The Southern Loop trail — earth path winding through thick jungle, cool and quiet at eight in the morning.

The loop is marked in yellow on the trail map — a winding circuit through the southern section of the park. As I walked it, I kept thinking about how Singapore has managed to hold on to pockets of nature like this, right inside the city, right next to people's homes. We are lucky. We do not always act like it, but we are.

The Reservoir

The View That Stopped Me Cold

Somewhere along the loop  ·  The moment that made the whole morning

And then the trees parted, and there it was.

I had been walking through dense jungle for a while — head down, watching the path, counting roots and noting the way the trail turned. And then the trail curved one final time and opened onto the reservoir, and I stopped walking entirely.

The water stretched out ahead of me, wide and calm and very, very still. The trees on the far bank were reflected in it so perfectly that for a moment the whole scene seemed doubled — the real world above, and its mirror image below, both equally clear, both equally green. The morning sky, still pale at that hour, lay on top of all of it like a second ceiling. No wind. No sound except birds.

The reservoir — perfectly still in the morning light, the tree line reflected like a mirror on the water.

I stood there for a long time. Not because I was tired — I was not tired — but because the view demanded it. There are moments in nature that ask you to stop and pay attention, and this was one of them. The water, the trees, the light, the silence. It was the kind of beautiful that you feel in your chest rather than just see with your eyes.

I have travelled to a lot of places in my life. I have stood at the edges of famous lakes and famous rivers and famous coastlines. And this reservoir, tucked into a park five minutes from my house in Singapore, was as beautiful as any of them. That surprised me. It also made me very happy.

"The water was perfectly still, reflecting the trees on the far bank like a mirror. I stood there for a long time, not because I needed to rest, but because some views ask you to stop. This was one of them."

Morning light on the water — the kind of scene you do not expect to find five minutes from home.

Two Hours Well Spent

8,500 Steps, and Feeling Strong

End of the loop  ·  Back at the trailhead  ·  10:00 AM

I completed the full Southern Loop in two hours. When I reached the trailhead again and checked my step count, the number read 8,500 steps. That is not a bad morning's work for any age. At seventy, it feels like a statement.

I did not feel beaten up by it. I felt the opposite — that particular kind of energy that comes from having worked your body properly in the open air. The muscles in my legs were warm and used. My breathing was steady. Everything felt right. This is what I mean when I say I feel very fit now: not young again — I know I am not young — but genuinely, measurably well. Moving well. Thinking clearly. Present in my body in a way that sitting still never gives me.

I retired at sixty-seven. I am seventy now. Three years into retirement, and I am doing two-hour solo jungle hikes before the morning is half done. I find this extraordinary, and I find it completely normal, in equal measure. That is the gift of doing this regularly. You stop seeing it as remarkable and start seeing it as simply what Tuesday morning looks like. Or Monday. Or whatever day it is when you step out the door and say: yes, let us go.

"8,500 steps. Two hours in the jungle. A reservoir I will not forget in a hurry. Alone with the birds and the trees and the sound of water. At seventy, that is not a small thing. That is everything."

🌿 What I Know About Chestnut Nature Park (Southern Loop)

  1. Go early. Eight o'clock is ideal — cool air, soft light, almost no one on the trail. The park fills up as the morning goes on.
  2. The loop is well-marked. The trail map at the entrance is accurate. Yellow markings on the Southern Loop. You will not get lost.
  3. Don't rush the reservoir. When the water appears, stop. Sit if there is somewhere to sit. Look at it properly. It is the best part of the whole trail.
  4. Bring water. Two hours in a Singapore jungle, even in the morning, requires hydration. There are no vending machines inside the park.
  5. Wear proper shoes. The trail has roots, mud patches, and uneven ground. Trail shoes or good running shoes, not sandals.
  6. Solo is fine. Tell someone where you are going (I sent Joanne a quick text) and take your phone. But going alone is peaceful and completely safe on this trail.

What It Means

Retired at 67, Still Exploring at 70

A thought at the end of a very good morning

I walked into a park I had never properly explored, alone, at eight in the morning, at the age of seventy. I walked for two hours through real jungle. I stood beside a reservoir that was so beautiful it made me go quiet. I came home feeling better than when I left.

This is what retirement looks like when you refuse to let it become small. Not every day is a grand adventure — some days are errands and doctors and all the ordinary furniture of life. But the days when I lace up my shoes and step out into the green — those days belong entirely to me, and they are among the best days I have had.

If you have never walked the Chestnut Nature Park Southern Loop, go. Go soon. Go alone if you can — the silence is part of what makes it special. And when you reach the reservoir, stop for a while. Let the view do what it needs to do.

You will not regret it.
