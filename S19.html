The Decision

Eight O'Clock in the Morning, and I Said Yes早上八点，我说了好

8 June 2026  ·  Jungle trail to the summit

The alarm went off at seven. By eight o'clock I was already at the trailhead, lacing up my shoes in the early morning cool, looking up at a wall of green so thick it swallowed the light. The jungle here is alive with sound — birds calling from somewhere deep in the canopy, insects already at work, the faint rustling of things you do not see. It is a place that makes you feel very small, and very glad to be there.

I am seventy years old. I retired at sixty-seven, and one of the gifts retirement gave me was permission to say yes to things I used to put off. No more waiting for the right time. Today was the right time.

"At seventy, you do not climb a summit to prove something. You climb it to remind yourself what you are still capable of."

The Climb

Steep Steps, Metal Rails, and One Stubborn Old Man陡峭的台阶、金属扶手，和一个固执的老头

The trail up  ·  Wooden steps into the canopy

The trail started gently enough — a packed earth path winding between the roots of enormous trees. Then came the steps. Wooden boards bolted into the hillside, with metal handrails on each side, climbing steeply up through the green dark. Each flight levelled out, caught its breath, and then turned up again.

I took it slowly. That is the whole secret, and I offer it freely: slow is not stopping. I paused when I needed to, took in the view through the trees, watched the light filter down through the leaves overhead, and then kept going. There is no shame in taking a breath at seventy. There is only shame in giving up — and I did not give up.

The trail up — steep steps cutting through dense jungle. Every step earned.

Looking up the stairway from below, you cannot see where it ends. The steps just disappear into the green. That is either terrifying or motivating, depending on the kind of person you are. I decided, standing there at the bottom with my hands on the rails, that it was motivating.

The jungle closed in on both sides. Ferns brushed my arms. The air smelled of damp earth and living things. Far above, somewhere I could not yet see, was the top. I kept climbing.

My Daily Trail

Dairy Farm Summit — 3,000 Steps Before Breakfast乳农园山顶——早餐前爬3000级台阶

Every morning  ·  Near home  ·  Dairy Farm Nature Park

This is not just a one-off adventure. I hike up to Dairy Farm Summit near my house every morning, and it is one of the best decisions I have made in retirement. The trail is 3,000 steps — not a stroll, not a warm-up. By the time I reach the top, I am properly sweating, my heart is going, and my legs have been given a real workout. That is the whole point. At seventy, you do not stay strong by accident. You train.

But the 3,000 steps are only part of it. What keeps me coming back, morning after morning, is everything else along the route. I pass beneath beautiful, enormous trees — old ones, deep-rooted, their canopies so high they seem to belong to a different world than the city below. I hear the birds singing from the moment I step onto the trail. Not recorded, not through earphones — real birds, close by, going about their morning as if I were just another creature passing through.

The trail at Dairy Farm — tall trees, dappled morning light, and the climb waiting above.

The smell of the forest is something you cannot replicate anywhere else. Damp earth, fallen leaves, living wood — the jungle breathes, and when you are standing in the middle of it in the early morning quiet, you breathe with it. It slows everything down in the best possible way.

Along the route — trees so old and tall they make the city feel very far away.

And then there is something harder to explain, but I will try. Out here, away from the screens and the concrete and the WiFi and the traffic, I can feel a very different kind of energy. A strong magnetic field — that is the best way I can describe it. A stillness, an aliveness, a sense that the natural world is doing something that we mostly walk straight past. I do not walk past it. I stop, I breathe, I feel it. It is one of the real reasons I come.

The trail opens up — light through the canopy, birdsong everywhere, and the forest alive around you.

My goal each morning is simple: hike all the way up to the summit, or — on the mornings my legs argue back — at the very least clock 10,000 steps. Some days I make the top with breath to spare. Some days I settle for the count. Either way I have moved, I have sweated, and I have been outdoors among the trees. At seventy, that is a morning well spent.

Near the summit — the climb behind me, the city below, and morning air that smells of real forest.

"3,000 steps. Trees that have been standing longer than I have been alive. Birds that do not know I am retired. A forest that does not care what I used to do. That is exactly what I need every morning."

The Summit

When the Steps Finally Ended

Top of the trail  ·  Worth every step

And then, quite suddenly, the steps ended. The trail levelled. The trees opened. I was at the top.

I stood there for a moment and just breathed. Not just because I needed to catch my breath — though I did — but because the view demanded it. The jungle rolled out below me in every direction, a great ocean of green under the morning sky. A breeze came up from somewhere and cooled my face. The birds were louder here, more confident at the top of their world.

I thought about the people who told me, kindly and with good intentions, that I should take it easy at my age. I thought about every time I have heard some version of "you're too old for that now." And I stood at the top of a jungle summit at eight-something in the morning, at seventy years old, and smiled to myself.

"The steps just disappeared into the green. You could not see the top from the bottom. That is probably the best kind of challenge — the kind you have to commit to before you can see how it ends."

I took a long, slow look at everything around me. The haze over the distant hills. The canopy swaying in the breeze below my feet. A bird I could not name gliding in a wide circle on a thermal, completely at ease with the air beneath its wings. I knew exactly how it felt.

🌿 What I've Learned About Hiking at 70

  1. Start early. The morning cool is your best friend. By midday the heat will win every argument — don't let it. Eight o'clock is perfect.
  2. Slow is not stopping. Pace yourself without apology. There is no prize for finishing first; there is only the summit or not the summit.
  3. Use the handrails. Pride is not worth a twisted ankle. The rails are there — use them freely and gratefully.
  4. Rest when you need to. Stop, look around, take in where you are. The pauses are part of the climb, not interruptions to it.
  5. Tell someone where you are going. A simple text to Joanne before I set off. It is common sense, not weakness.
  6. The view at the top is better because of the steps that got you there. A summit you drove to is a nice view. A summit you climbed is something else entirely.

What It Means

Retired at 67, Climbing at 70

A reflection at the top of the world

I retired at sixty-seven. Three years on, I am more active than I was at fifty-five. Retirement, for me, did not mean slowing down — it meant being freed to go faster in the directions that actually matter.

The jungle does not care how old you are. The steps do not get easier because you have a good excuse. But they also do not refuse you. They just sit there, waiting, for whoever is willing to climb them. Today I was willing. Today I was the person standing at the bottom at eight in the morning, looking up at a trail that disappeared into the green, and saying: yes.

If you are reading this and you have been putting something off — a hike, a trip, an adventure, a challenge that feels too big — let me be the voice that says: go. Go while you can. The steps are waiting. The summit is there.

You just have to say yes.

"The jungle does not care how old you are. The steps do not refuse you. They just wait for whoever is willing to climb."
