Patagonia — The Dream I Chased for Ten Years

From San Francisco to the end of the world — a 70-year-old adventurer's journey through Antarctica and the wild heart of Torres del Paine.

Torres del Paine, Chile Antarctica · 12 Days Solo Travel

Chapter One

A Dream Planted Ten Years Ago

Ten years ago, someone told me about a mountain. They said it was one of the most beautiful places on earth. I filed it away — the kind of dream you carry quietly, not sure if you'll ever get there.

In December 2024, I went solo from San Francisco all the way south — through Ushuaia, across the Drake Passage to Antarctica for twelve extraordinary days on the ice, and finally to Patagonia. By the time I reached Torres del Paine, that decade-old dream was about to become real.

Double thumbs up — the iconic three towers visible behind me. A dream finally standing right there.

Puerto Natales was my base. A small, windswept town at the edge of Chilean Patagonia — the kind of place where serious hikers converge, where you sort your gear and study the trail maps and feel the electricity of what's coming. I fell in love with it immediately. If I had the chance to return, I would stay there for a full month.

Chapter Two

Into the Park

Left: Setting off with a fellow hiker — the towers already visible on the horizon. Right: The jagged granite that gives the park its otherworldly character.

Torres del Paine National Park is not a gentle place. The wind comes at you sideways, the weather turns on a dime, and the terrain demands everything you have. But from the moment I crossed into the park, I understood why people travel halfway around the world to be here.

The mountains rise from the steppe like something from another planet — vast granite towers sculpted by glaciers over millions of years, streaked with minerals that catch the light in shades of copper and gold and grey. Los Cuernos (The Horns) alone stopped me in my tracks repeatedly.

Los Cuernos — the distinctive dark-capped horns that make Torres del Paine unmistakable.

I had heard for ten years that this was one of the most beautiful places in the world. Standing there, trekking poles in hand, I understood. Some places are even bigger than the words people use to describe them.

— Paul, December 2024
Chapter Three

Las Torres — Ten Hours Up

The hike to the base of Las Torres — the three iconic granite spires — is the crown jewel of the park. I set out early and climbed for ten hours.

The trail takes you through lenga beech forests, past roaring streams, over boulder fields, and finally up a steep final scramble of loose rocks. Every step higher reveals a new dimension of the landscape. Your legs ache. The wind pushes back. And then you reach the top.

The moment ten years of dreaming came true — Las Torres reflected in the impossibly turquoise glacial lake below.

The three towers soar above a turquoise glacial lake, the water so vivid it looks photoshopped. You stand there, breathless from the climb and the sight, and everything — the years of anticipation, the long journey south, the ten hours of hiking — collapses into this one perfect moment.

Arms up, ten hours in — made it to the top! That feeling never gets old.

Chapter Four

Lakes of Glass and Ice

Patagonia is a water world. The lakes here have colors that don't seem to belong to nature — glacial milk-turquoise, stormy steel, vivid cyan. And they change depending on light, cloud, and wind.

Lago Pehoé — the boat ride across this lake offered some of the most cinematic scenery of the entire journey.

The boat ride across Lago Pehoé was one of the most scenic moments of the entire trip. Surrounded by mountains on all sides, the water a vivid mineral blue, the boat cutting through chop under a sky that shifts every few minutes — it felt like traveling through a painting.

Lago Grey was a different mood entirely — colder, more dramatic, the glacier pushing blue-white ice directly into the lake. I stood at the Lago Grey sign, bundled up against the wind, the massive Grey Glacier visible behind me in the mist.

Lago Grey — standing on the viewing rock above the glacier-fed lake, icebergs drifting below.

Vivid blue icebergs calved from the Grey Glacier floating on the milky-green lake — one of the most surreal sights I've ever witnessed.

Chapter Five

Sleeping Under Canvas

Most nights in the park, I slept in a tent — simple, honest, cold, and absolutely worth it.

Left: My trusty Salewa tent — home for the nights. Right: Camp life — cooking on a small gas stove in the communal tent at the campsite.

The campsites inside Torres del Paine are rustic but functional. You share the cooking shelter with other trekkers from all over the world — Chileans, Europeans, Australians, Americans — everyone comparing blisters and marveling at what they've just seen. The communal energy is part of the experience.

Camping in Torres del Paine — What to Know

Permits are required and must be booked months in advance. The W Circuit and O Circuit require reservations at each campsite. Nights are cold even in December (Patagonian summer), so a good sleeping bag is essential. Most campsites have basic cooking shelters and bathrooms. The communal atmosphere is one of the unexpected joys.

Chapter Six

Wild Things — Flora and Fauna

The park teems with life. The ghost-white lenga beech trees — bleached by fire and frost, their bare branches spreading like skeletal hands against the sky — are an art form in themselves. The flora underfoot includes tiny wildflowers that somehow thrive in the rock and wind.

Left to right: Dead lenga beech trees bleached white by fire — hauntingly beautiful. A wildflower — possibly a Calceolaria — growing in the rocky scrub.

A bird of prey — likely a Chimango Caracara — perched calmly on a fallen log while the clouds rolled in behind.

The park's wildlife is unhurried and unafraid. Guanacos trot across the trail. Condors wheel overhead. And sometimes, a falcon simply sits and watches you pass, as unbothered by your presence as if you were just another piece of the landscape.

Chapter Seven

From Ushuaia — The End of the World

Before Patagonia came Ushuaia — Argentina's southernmost city, dramatically nestled between snow-capped mountains and the Beagle Channel. From here, ships depart for Antarctica. And from here, I set sail.

The pier at Ushuaia — moody, atmospheric, and utterly at the end of the earth. From here, ships cross the Drake Passage to Antarctica.

Twelve days on the Antarctic ice is a story in itself. The silence, the scale, the absolute otherness of it — a white continent where time seems to work differently. I came back changed. And then I came to Patagonia, which felt, by contrast, positively warm and lush.

The ferry across Lago Pehoé, and the rustic lodge at the lake's edge — a welcome sight after long days on the trail.

Puerto Natales is one of those places that wraps around you. If I had the chance to go back, I would stay for a month — hiking, reading, watching the weather roll in off the mountains, and just being still in one of the most beautiful corners of the world.

— Paul, reflecting on Torres del Paine, December 2024
Chapter Eight

What a Dream Feels Like

I'm not sure exactly when the dream shifted from something abstract to something real. Maybe it was standing at the Lago Grey sign, the wind trying to knock me sideways. Maybe it was that first glimpse of the towers above the treeline. Maybe it was the moment I stepped off the final boulder and looked up at Las Torres for the first time.

What I know is this: ten years of a dream doesn't diminish when it comes true. It amplifies. The towers were bigger than the stories. The lakes were more vivid than the photos. The silence was more absolute than I'd imagined.

Patagonia doesn't disappoint. It exceeds.
