Swing Trading the Singapore Market
A Strategy I'm Learning

At 70, a good friend is teaching me how to ride the market's short waves — here's what I'm learning, in plain English.

"I am 70 years old and still learning. My friend Fred Tay sent me two photos on WhatsApp — pages from a swing-trading course — and offered to teach me. I was curious, but also cautious. The stock market has humbled wiser men than me. So I decided to do what I always do: write it all down in plain words, so I truly understand it before I ever risk a single dollar."

🌊 What Is Swing Trading?

Fred explained it to me simply. Day trading means buying and selling within the same day — chasing the market minute by minute. That sounded far too stressful for me. Swing trading is gentler: you hold a stock for a few days — in this strategy, up to 5 days — to catch one short "swing" in price, then you step away.

You are not glued to the screen all day. You set up your trade, you know exactly when you'll get in, and you know exactly when you'll get out — whether it goes well or badly. For a retiree like me, that discipline is the appeal. The plan is decided before the emotion arrives.

💡 The Big Idea

Don't try to predict the market. Wait for the chart to line up into a known pattern, enter at a precise trigger, and always know your exit before you begin.

🧭 The Three Tools on the Chart

This strategy uses just three indicators. Here's how Fred described them to me, in everyday language:

Tool 1

📏 The 20MA

The 20-period Simple Moving Average — the average price over the last 20 bars. It's the faster, more sensitive line. Think of it as the stock's recent mood.

Tool 2

📐 The 40MA

The 40-period Simple Moving Average — the average over 40 bars. It's the slower line, the bigger trend. Think of it as the stock's overall direction.

Tool 3

🌡️ The CCI (±100)

The Commodity Channel Index measures how stretched the price is. Below −100 means it's been pushed down hard (possibly oversold); above +100 means pushed up hard (possibly overbought). These extremes are our cue to pay attention.

The clever part: the two moving averages tell us the trend, while the CCI tells us when the price has stretched far enough to snap back. We only act when both agree.

🟢 The Swing BUY Set-Up (Target Up)

This is the bullish pattern — we expect the price to bounce up. Fred says all five conditions should be present, like a checklist, before you even think about buying:

✅ Buy Checklist — all 5 must line up
  • The moving average is sloping up (the trend is rising).
  • The 20MA is above the 40MA (the fast line is leading higher).
  • The CCI dips below −100 (price got stretched down — a pullback).
  • The price's low touches the 20MA, or dips just slightly below it.
  • The candle closes above the 40MA (the bigger trend still holds).
🔥 FIRE — Buy Trigger Buy 1 bid above the prior day's high. You only enter if the price proves itself by pushing past yesterday's top.
🛑 IA — Stop Loss Sell 1 bid below the prior day's low. If the pattern fails, you exit small and live to trade another day.

A "bid" is simply one price step on the Singapore Exchange's tick ladder — one tiny notch above or below.

🔴 The Swing SELL Set-Up (Target Down)

This is the exact mirror image — the bearish pattern, where we expect the price to fall. (In Singapore this usually means short-selling, which is more advanced — Fred warned me to learn the buy side properly first.)

⛔ Sell Checklist — all 5 must line up
  • The moving average is sloping down (the trend is falling).
  • The 20MA is below the 40MA (the fast line is leading lower).
  • The CCI rises above +100 (price got stretched up — a bounce).
  • The price's high touches the 20MA, or pokes just above it.
  • The candle closes below the 40MA (the downtrend still holds).
🔥 FIRE — Sell Trigger Sell 1 bid below the prior day's low. You only enter the short if price breaks under yesterday's bottom.
🛑 IA — Stop Loss Buy 1 bid above the prior day's high. If it turns against you, you cover and accept the small loss.
· · ·

🎯 The Golden Rule: Trigger In, Stop Out

The part Fred kept repeating to me is this: the set-up alone is not a reason to trade. You wait for the FIRE trigger — the price must break past the previous day's high (to buy) or low (to sell) before you act. The market has to confirm the move itself.

And the very moment you enter, you place your IA stop loss. This is non-negotiable. The stop is decided before the trade, when your head is clear — not in the heat of a losing position when fear and hope take over.

"Must remember to put your stop loss." — the first thing Fred told me, and the thing he kept saying.

⚠️ The Part That Matters Most: Risk

Fred was honest with me, and I respect him for it. He didn't sell me a dream of easy money. His warnings were as important as the strategy itself:

🛑 Fred's Cautions (read these twice)

  • Always put your stop loss. Never enter a trade without knowing exactly where you'll get out if you're wrong.
  • This is very stressful — practise on a paper account first. Use a demo/paper-trading account and prove the strategy works for you, with no real money on the line, before you ever risk a real dollar.
  • Only risk what you can afford to lose. Never trade with money you need for living, medical care, or peace of mind.
  • Small losses are part of the plan. Even a good strategy loses sometimes. The stop loss keeps each loss small so one bad trade can't hurt you.

For me, at 70, that last point is everything. I am not trying to get rich. I am learning a skill, slowly and carefully, the same way I'm learning AI. Capital preservation comes first. The thrill of a winning trade is never worth losing my peace.
