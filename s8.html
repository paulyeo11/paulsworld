S8: The Man Behind the Lens

"The wind knows where to find me."

Somewhere on a shoreline — camera ready, heart open.

There is a photograph of me that I did not take. That is the strange thing about it. I am always the one behind the lens — searching for the light, waiting for the moment, pressing the shutter just as the world offers up something true. But in this image, I am the subject, and the world caught me off-guard.

It was a windswept day by the sea. I remember the cold biting through my red jacket, the kind of cold that makes your eyes water and your cheeks flush. I had my Canon around my neck — my constant companion on every trip — and I was smiling. Not a posed smile for a camera pointed at me, but the real kind: the smile of a man who is exactly where he wants to be.

"A camera is not just a machine. It is a way of paying attention — a promise to the world that you are watching, that you care."

I came to photography late in life, the way I came to many things I love. Travel, adventure, writing — these were not the pursuits of my younger years, when work and responsibility filled every corner of my days. But retirement opened a door I had not known was there, and I walked through it with a camera in my hand.

What I have learned is this: the best photographs are never really about the subject. They are about the relationship between the photographer and the moment. That split second when you decide — yes, this, now. The mountains of Xinjiang at dawn. The temples of Kyoto in rain. A stranger's face in a Hong Kong market. These images live in me long after they have been saved to a card and uploaded to a screen.

My wife Joanne has often said that I am more patient with a camera than with anything else in life. She is probably right. I can wait an hour for the right light over a lake. I can crouch at an uncomfortable angle for the perfect frame. The camera teaches me things that nothing else quite manages — stillness, attention, gratitude for what is already there.

"Every journey I take, I bring two things: an open itinerary and a full memory card."

In this photograph, the wind is in my hair and the sea is behind me, and I am grinning like a man who has discovered, at seventy, that life keeps offering new things to see. I hope that is what people notice when they look at it. Not the grey beard or the red jacket — but the aliveness. The sense that somewhere out there, there is another frame waiting to be found.

I am not done looking.
