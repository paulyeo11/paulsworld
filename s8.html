S8: The Speech I Almost Didn't Give

Stories · Paul's World

I stood at the back of the room holding a printed speech I'd rewritten four times, and I was seriously considering walking out before my name got called.

Here's the thing nobody warns you about retirement: you think you're done proving yourself. Then you join a Toastmasters club that runs entirely in Mandarin, and at seventy years old you're back to feeling like the new kid who doesn't know where anything is. My Mandarin is functional — good enough for a market, a taxi, a bowl of noodles. It is not, I promise you, good enough for a seven-minute speech on leading through difficult situations, delivered from memory, in front of people half my age who've been speaking the language since birth.

I'd picked the topic myself, which in hindsight was either brave or foolish. Leading in difficult situations. I know something about that — thirty-odd years of work taught me plenty about hard calls and harder conversations. But translating lived experience into a foreign tongue is its own kind of difficult situation, and I hadn't fully appreciated the irony until I was three drafts in, crossing out sentences that sounded fine in my head and completely wrong out loud.

My club mates were kind about it, the way people are kind to someone clearly out of his depth. One of them, a woman maybe thirty years younger than me, sat with me for an hour going line by line, fixing my tones, laughing gently at the ones I'd mangled beyond recognition. I remember feeling embarrassed and grateful in almost equal measure — that particular retiree's cocktail of humility you don't get much of when you're still working and everyone defers to your title.

When my name was called I walked up there sure I'd forget everything. And for about ten seconds, standing at the front with the lights a little too bright, I very nearly did. Then I found the first line, the one I'd practiced in the shower and on my bike and half-asleep in bed, and it came out. Not perfect. My tones wobbled in at least three places, and I know I said "difficult" wrong the entire speech because nobody corrected me until afterward. But it came out, all seven minutes of it, and I got to the end still standing.

The applause afterward wasn't for eloquence. It was, I think, for the fact that a seventy-year-old man had chosen the hard road on purpose, when nobody would have blinked if I'd picked the easy one — or skipped the whole thing entirely and stuck to English, or golf, or simply resting on the fact that I'd already done my working life's worth of hard things.

The lesson I keep relearning, on bikes, on mountains, and apparently at a lectern in a language not my own: the fear never fully goes away, and it doesn't need to. You just need one line you've practiced enough to say first. The rest of the speech takes care of itself.
