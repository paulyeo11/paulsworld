TUT03: How to Write a Travel Book with Claude — From Journal Notes to Finished Manuscript

A step-by-step guide to turning your travel memories into a publish-ready book using AI

Paul Yeo  ·  June 2026  ·  Tutorials Series

Every story on this website started as scattered notes — a few lines jotted on my phone after a hike, a voice memo in the car, a string of photos with no caption. Claude is what turned that mess into finished chapters, in my own voice. Here is exactly how I do it.

· · ·

Step 1 — Gather Your Raw Material First

Do not start by asking Claude to "write a travel book." Start by collecting everything you already have: journal entries, WhatsApp messages to family, photo captions, even half-finished notes app entries. None of it needs to be polished. Paste it all into one place — a single document per trip is enough.

· · ·

Step 2 — Teach Claude Your Voice

The single biggest mistake people make is letting Claude write in its own generic voice. Instead, give it 2-3 examples of things you have already written — an email, a journal entry, even a long text message — and say:

Here are three examples of how I naturally write about my travels. Study the tone, sentence length, and the kind of detail I notice. From now on, write in this voice — not a generic travel-blog voice.

— A starting prompt you can copy and edit

This is exactly how I built SWEETIE, the persona I use to turn my own travel memories into chapters — she does not invent a new voice, she studies mine and writes as me, just organised.

· · ·

Step 3 — Build the Chapter Outline Together

Before any writing happens, ask Claude to help you decide the shape of the book. Two common structures:

Two Ways to Structure a Travel Book

1
Chronological One chapter per day or per stop on the trip. Easiest to write, reads like a journey unfolding in real time. Good for a single trip with a clear start and end.
2
Thematic Chapters grouped by theme — "Food," "People I Met," "Close Calls," "What Surprised Me." Good if you are combining memories from several trips into one book.

Ask Claude: "Here are my raw notes from this trip. Suggest a chapter outline — chronological or thematic, whichever fits better — and tell me why." Let it propose; you decide.

· · ·

Step 4 — Draft Chapter by Chapter, Then Edit Yourself

Feed Claude your raw notes for one chapter at a time and ask it to expand them into narrative prose, in your voice, keeping every true detail. Then read it carefully and edit. This step matters: AI can organise and expand, but only you know if a memory feels true. Change anything that does not sound like you.

AI gave me a first draft in minutes. But the book only became mine once I went through every chapter and put my own words back where Claude had smoothed over something that mattered to me.

— Why the editing pass is not optional
· · ·

Step 5 — Add Structure and Photos

Once chapters are drafted, ask Claude to help with the supporting pieces: a table of contents, a short introduction, chapter titles that match your style. If you are including photos, follow the same rule used across this whole website — reduce image size, fix orientation, and keep every image embedded directly in the file rather than linked externally, so the finished book works even offline.

· · ·

Step 6 — Export the Finished Book

A simple, durable way to finish is to keep the whole book as one HTML file (or one HTML file per chapter, linked together) with all images embedded. From there it can be converted to PDF or another ebook format using any standard converter. The important habit is to keep one self-contained master file you control, rather than scattering chapters across separate apps.

· · ·

Usage Tips

  • 1Collect raw notes before you write anything. Editing existing material is far easier than starting from a blank page.
  • 2Give Claude real writing samples so it studies your voice instead of inventing a generic one.
  • 3Work one chapter at a time. Trying to draft an entire book in one prompt produces something shallow and generic.
  • 4Always do a personal editing pass. Change anything that does not feel true to your memory.
  • 5Keep photos embedded, not linked, so your finished book works even without an internet connection.
· · ·

Q&A

Q: Will the book sound like Claude wrote it, not me?
A: Only if you skip Step 2 and Step 4. Giving real writing samples and doing a personal editing pass on every chapter are what keep the voice yours.
Q: How much of my own notes do I need before starting?
A: More than you think you have. Even scattered text messages and photo captions add up to a surprising amount of raw material once collected in one place.
Q: Can I publish this book and sell it?
A: That depends on your own goals and on the terms of whatever publishing platform you use — review their guidelines on AI-assisted writing before publishing commercially.
Q: I am not a "writer." Can this still work for me?
A: Yes — this whole website is proof. The notes do not need to be polished. Claude organises and expands; you supply the memories and the final editing eye.
· · ·

Final Thoughts

I did not set out to "write a book." I set out to stop losing memories that were scattered across notes apps and old messages. The book became the natural result of organising what I already had, with Claude doing the heavy lifting of structure and expansion, and me doing the heavy lifting of making sure it still sounded like me.

Your Starting Point

Open your phone's notes app right now and copy every scrap you have written about one single trip into a new document. That document is chapter one, waiting to be expanded.
