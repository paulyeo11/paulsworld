How It Started

It crept up on me quietly. One morning I woke up and the world sounded like I was underwater. My right ear felt full — like someone had stuffed cotton wool deep inside. I kept tilting my head, yawning, swallowing. Nothing helped.

At first I didn't think much of it. Maybe I slept on it funny. Maybe a bit of water from the shower. But days went by and nothing changed. Conversations became harder. I kept asking people to repeat themselves. Watching TV, I had to turn the volume up much higher than Joanne liked.

Three weeks of muffled sound. It's amazing how much you take clear hearing for granted — until it's gone.

What the Doctor Did

1
Ear inspection with otoscope
A small lit scope to see inside the ear canal and confirm earwax impaction (cerumen impaction).
2
Micro-suction
A tiny vacuum-like device removes the wax plug safely without pushing it further in. Preferred over ear syringing (water irrigation) as it's gentler and more precise.
3
Done in under 1 minute
No pain, no water, no fuss. Just a little suction sound, and then crystal-clear hearing returned immediately.

What I Learned

Earwax (cerumen) is natural and protective. It traps dust and bacteria and normally works its way out on its own. But as we get older, earwax can become drier and harder, and the self-cleaning mechanism slows down. For people who are active — especially hikers and swimmers — sweat and humidity can soften wax and push it deeper.

The biggest mistake people make — myself included — is using cotton buds. They don't remove wax; they push it deeper and compact it, making a blockage worse. I've now thrown all our cotton buds away (much to Joanne's protest).

🌿 My Ear Care Rules Going Forward

  • No cotton buds — ever. The ear canal is self-cleaning.
  • If my ear feels blocked for more than 3 days, see a doctor — don't wait 3 weeks.
  • Once a year ear check at the GP, especially since I hike and sweat a lot.
  • Olive oil drops (2–3 drops, 3–4 days) can help soften wax early on.
  • After swimming, tilt head and let water drain — don't dig with anything.

A Moment of Gratitude

Standing outside the clinic, I just listened. The air conditioner hum, a car passing, a child laughing somewhere — all of it crystal clear. Three weeks of muffled sound makes you realise how precious normal hearing is.

At 70, I'm learning that good health isn't just about big things — the heart, the joints, the blood pressure. It's also about the small, silent things we take for granted every single day. Hearing. Sight. Smell. Things that just work — until they don't.
