Pearl of the Orient — 27 Days Across the Philippines

Paul and Jeremy's grand adventure through the archipelago: mountain peaks, ancient forts, island sunsets, and the finest tuna on earth.

Manila Baguio Cebu Bohol General Santos Puerto Princesa El Nido Coron Boracay
The Journey Begins

Two Old Friends, One Archipelago

27 October – 23 November 2023  ·  27 Days

Sometimes, the best adventures begin with a simple decision: why not? My friend Jeremy and I had been talking about the Philippines for years. A country of over 7,000 islands — a riot of colour, faith, food, and natural beauty — it had always seemed both close and impossibly vast. In October 2023, we finally said enough planning, let's go.

Twenty-seven days. Nine destinations. Buses, jeepneys, ferries, mountain trails, underground rivers, and the most beautiful sunsets either of us had ever witnessed. This is our story.

Chapter 1

Manila — Walled City, Street Bowls & Colonial Cobblestones

27 – 29 October  ·  City Garden Grand Hotel

We landed in Manila brimming with excitement. The city hits you immediately — the heat, the honking, the chaos and colour that somehow coheres into something alive. We dropped our bags at the City Garden Grand Hotel and headed straight out into the streets.

A local carenderia in Manila's streets — pares, bulalo, mami, and beef brain stew bubbling in giant pots. We ate here on our first evening, elbows on the shared counter, Pepsi in hand.

Our first meal set the tone for the whole trip. We found a tiny streetside stall near Guadalupe — Alyssa Paresan — where a stocky cook in a sleeveless shirt stirred enormous cauldrons of bulalo and beef pares. The broth was deeply savoury, the beef meltingly tender. This was the real Manila, not the mall food court version.

The next morning we rode a jeepney across the city. I gripped the overhead rail as we lurched through traffic, crammed in next to locals, bags piled on our laps. Jeremy had ridden a jeepney before but for me, first timer, this was pure exhilaration — loud, colourful, impossibly efficient.

Riding the jeepney — Manila's original mass transit. I'm the one in the middle trying to look like I do this every day.

We spent a long afternoon in Intramuros, the old walled city the Spanish built in the 16th century. Walking those stone streets felt like stepping out of modern Manila entirely. We stopped in front of Casa Manila, posed for a photo on the cobblestones, then followed the ancient walls to Fort Santiago.

Outside Casa Manila — Jeremy and I feeling very colonial.
At Fort Santiago, where centuries of Philippine history are held in crumbling stone.

Inside Fort Santiago, we explored the old underground tunnels — damp, mossy corridors where Spanish soldiers and, later, Filipino prisoners once walked. Jeremy ducked his head through the low entrance; I followed, torchlight casting shadows on the ancient volcanic rock.

Inside the tunnels of Fort Santiago — a sobering reminder of the Philippines' colonial history.

Chapter 2

Baguio — The Summer Capital & A Mountain Summit

29 October – 1 November  ·  Microtel by Wyndham Baguio

We caught the bus north to Baguio from the city terminal — the real kind, with punch-card tickets and a uniformed conductor checking every seat. Jeremy photographed our ticket like a tourist, which I teased him about, but honestly — I did the same.

The Baguio terminal — crowded, busy, and wonderfully chaotic.
Our bus ticket — a punch-card relic of another era.
On the coach to Baguio. Six-hour journey, golden-yellow seats, mountain switchbacks ahead.

Baguio greeted us with cool mountain air — a startling, welcome contrast to the heat of Manila. Called the Summer Capital of the Philippines, it sits at 1,500 metres in the Cordillera mountains, ringed by pine trees and morning mist. We immediately felt the altitude in our lungs, in a good way.

Our highlight was an early morning hike to Mount Ulap. We set off before dawn with trekking poles, and the trail rewarded us magnificently. By mid-morning we were standing on the rocky summit, two silver-haired retirees with their arms outstretched, the entire Cordillera range spread before us in blue-green waves.

Summit of Mount Ulap, Baguio — two men, two walking sticks, and an entire mountain range to ourselves.

Jeremy always says age is just a number. Standing on that exposed rocky outcrop, the valley dropping away hundreds of metres below us, I believed him completely. We felt ridiculously, deliriously alive.

On the edge of the world at Mount Ulap — this photo took more nerve than it looks.

"Standing on that summit, arms wide, the Cordillera mountains rolling away in every direction — I thought: this is exactly why we travel. This is what retirement is for."

The descent took us past the 9 KM marker on the trail — we paused for a triumphant thumbs-up photo. A local hiker in a wide-brimmed hat was our companion for the last stretch down, swapping stories about the best trails in the region.

9 kilometres into the trail — still smiling, which surprised us both.
Coming down from the summit — the Cordillera steps carved into the hillside.

Chapter 3

Cebu — Sunsets, Lechon, and the Tuna Capital of the World

2 – 5 November  ·  Vista Mar Beach Resort

Back briefly through Manila, then south by plane to Cebu — the Queen City of the South. We checked into Vista Mar Beach Resort, where pink bougainvillea tumbled over the wall towards a glittering sea, and the first sunset stopped us both mid-sentence.

The view from Vista Mar Beach Resort — bougainvillea and turquoise water. No filter needed.
Sunset over the Visayan Sea — the first of many that would leave us speechless.

Cebu is a city that feeds you well. At the local market we found coconut palm hearts — creamy white towers of fresh ubod — and every kind of street snack. But the undisputed king of Cebu cuisine is lechon: whole roasted pig, crackling skin lacquered deep amber, the most famous in the Philippines.

Cebu lechon — whole roasted pork in a glass display case. The skin crackles, the meat falls apart. Worth every calorie.
Fresh ubod (palm heart) at the Cebu market — a Visayan delicacy carved fresh daily.

We also found some delightful local company. Three young girls in traditional Sinulog festival costumes — red and white with gold trim and yellow flowers in their hair — posed shyly for us in a garden courtyard, then waved us off with enormous smiles. Jeremy said they were the most charming thing he'd seen all trip.

Three girls in traditional Sinulog festival dress — red, gold, and pure joy.

Jeremy had read about General Santos being the tuna capital of the Philippines, and nothing prepared us for the fish market at dawn. Row upon row of massive yellowfin tuna, some nearly two metres long, laid out on stainless steel tables, tagged and weighed. A vendor hoisted one by its tail — the thing was bigger than I am tall — and I genuinely could not believe this was breakfast entertainment.

A single yellowfin tuna, weighed and ready — bigger than any fish I've ever seen.
Dozens of giant tuna lined up at the General Santos fish market before 6am.

We had a magnificent dinner that same evening — fresh tuna sashimi, grilled tuna belly, and a restaurant so elegant it surprised both of us. Good food and good company: that's a theme I could repeat for the whole 27 days.

Dinner out in Cebu — Jeremy and I at our most content, empty plates and full hearts.

Chapter 4

Bohol — Chocolate Hills, Sand Castles & Alona Beach

5 – 8 November  ·  Panglao Regents Park Resort

From Cebu we crossed by ferry to Bohol — an island of gentle rivers, ancient churches, and one of the most extraordinary geological sights in Southeast Asia. The ferry arrived in brilliant morning sunshine, and we drove straight to the Chocolate Hills.

No photograph truly prepares you. Over 1,200 perfectly conical limestone hills, carpeted in grass that turns brown in dry season — hence the name. They roll away to the horizon in every direction, like nature's own geometry lesson. Tourists line the railing at the viewing platform, elbowing for photos. We simply stood there, staring.

The famous viewing platform — visitors clamouring for the perfect shot.
The Chocolate Hills — over 1,200 perfect cones stretching to the horizon.

We were also bowled over by the spiritual life of Bohol. Driving between sights, we passed a Divine Mercy shrine — a long staircase leading to a towering white statue of Jesus, arms outstretched, flanked by colourful angel figures and manicured gardens. There was something unexpectedly moving about it in the quiet morning light. Jeremy paused the car and we walked the steps in silence.

A Divine Mercy shrine on the road in Bohol — the stairway lined with statues, the figure of Christ at the summit.

Evenings were spent at Alona Beach in Panglao — a lively strip of white sand, thatched bars, and the constant sound of small waves. The sand castle at the entrance was a daily work of art: Alona Panglao Bohol, 11 06 23 carved in perfect block letters, a new one each day.

Sunset at Alona Beach, Panglao — the day's sand sculpture reads the date perfectly. A new one every morning.

The sunsets at Panglao were extraordinary — a mirror-flat sea reflecting towering indigo clouds, the horizon blushing orange and gold. Jeremy lounged in a beach hammock while I photographed him pretending to be asleep. He wasn't.

Alona Beach at dusk — boats pulled up, the crowd gathering to watch the sun go down.

On our last Bohol morning, before the boat to the next island, I sat alone by the water's edge. The sea was completely still — the kind of still that makes you feel the world has paused just for you. A local fishing boat sat motionless on the glassy surface; above it, a bruised purple cloudbank held the last of the sunrise's warmth. I photographed it and thought: nobody back home will believe this was just a random Tuesday morning.

Dawn on the water at Panglao — a mirror-calm sea, a fishing boat, and perfect silence.

Chapter 5

Puerto Princesa — The Underground River & Palawan's Jungle Heart

8 – 16 November  ·  General Santos → Puerto Princesa

After General Santos and its extraordinary tuna market — which genuinely deserves its own documentary — we flew west to Puerto Princesa in Palawan, one of the most beautiful provinces on earth.

We visited a local waterfall deep in the jungle — an enormous curtain of white water dropping into a bright green pool, mist rising into the tree canopy. We were the only visitors that morning. Standing before it, the spray cool on our faces, felt like stumbling onto a secret the world hadn't found yet.

A jungle waterfall near Puerto Princesa — a wall of white water in green silence.

The crown jewel of Puerto Princesa is the Puerto Princesa Subterranean River — a UNESCO World Heritage site and one of the New Seven Wonders of Nature. You board a small paddle boat, strap on a hard hat and life vest, and glide into a mountain carved by eight million years of flowing water.

Jeremy and I geared up — hard hats, life vests, and barely-contained excitement.
Emerging from the underground river — the jungle and sky waiting at the cave mouth.

We also encountered local culture that moved us. Filipino women in traditional dress — something that you might see at a community celebration or heritage event — waved cheerfully from a roadside garden, and it reminded us that the Philippines is not merely a tourist destination but a living, breathing culture with deep roots.

Traditional slow-cooked beef cuts — a regional specialty tied together with lemongrass.

On the first evening in Puerto Princesa, Jeremy and I found ourselves relaxing on a bamboo platform over a river estuary — the mangrove forest stretching away behind us, the water mirroring the blue evening sky. We sat in those woven recliners without saying a word for a long time. Two old friends, two weeks into an adventure, completely at peace.

Bamboo platform over the river estuary — the perfect place to do absolutely nothing.
Paul in a rattan egg chair at dusk — Mount Apo visible in the background. This is retirement.
Palawan at dusk — palm fronds silhouetted against a coral and violet sky.

Chapter 6

El Nido & Coron — Limestone Cliffs, Hidden Lagoons & Buddha by the Sea

16 – 22 November  ·  El Nido → Coron

El Nido is the kind of place that makes everything else seem ordinary afterwards. Towering karst limestone cliffs rise straight from the Sulu Sea, their faces draped in jungle. Hidden between them are secret beaches, emerald lagoons, and water so clear you can read the sand ten metres below.

We met Paul's local contact in General Santos, who had connected with us — two young women joined us at the pier for a farewell photo, laughing and relaxed, mountains and navy vessels in the background. The warmth of Filipino hospitality never stopped surprising me.

At the General Santos pier — a warm farewell with new friends before heading to Palawan.

Island-hopping around El Nido, we landed on hidden beaches tucked between vast limestone walls. The water was warm, crystalline, alive with reef fish. Our bangka boat crew knew every cove and secret passage.

A hidden beach in El Nido — limestone walls, a bangka at anchor, and no one else in sight.

Jeremy insisted on photographing everything through the dramatic rock formation at Matinloc Island — a natural arch in the cliff face through which you could see the clear sea and a waiting bangka. I did the same. Some frames are just too perfect to pass up.

The world framed in stone — a natural rock arch with a waiting bangka on the other side.

In Coron, Busuanga island, we encountered one of the most unexpected and beautiful sights of the whole journey: white Buddha statues seated on the rocks above the waterline, a thatched-roof villa rising behind them, the green mountain slopes as backdrop. The whole scene reflected in still water. Coron is famous for wreck diving, but moments like this are why you really come.

White Buddha figures on the rocks in Coron — one of the most serene sights of the entire journey.

One afternoon in Coron we also sat on the flattened coral rock ledges above the sea, watching local teenagers dive and splash in the turquoise shallows below, their laughter carrying up to us on the breeze. The blue stretched away forever.

Jeremy on another Cebu Pacific flight — every jump between islands was an adventure in itself.
✦ ✦ ✦
Chapter 7

Boracay — White Beach, Sailboats at Sunset, and the Sweetest Halo-Halo

22 November  ·  White Beach

Our final island stop was the one the whole world knows: Boracay. Four kilometres of powder-white sand and some of the finest beach sunsets on earth. We arrived midday, walked the full length of White Beach, then settled in to wait for what everyone here comes to see.

By late afternoon, everything turned gold. Orange loungers lined the sand in long rows; we chose two, ordered cold drinks, and let the evening arrive slowly. The sea was a dozen shades of blue — aquamarine close in, cobalt further out, midnight at the horizon.

White Beach at Boracay — orange loungers, a cloudy afternoon, and the sea ahead.

The iconic rock formation at the south end of the beach — a massive limestone shelf over the water, lone trees growing improbably from the flat top — is Boracay's most photographed natural feature. Kids jumped into the waves from the lower ledges as we watched from above, their splashing and shouting the perfect soundtrack.

The famous rock formation at White Beach — trees growing from limestone above the sea.
Local kids making the most of the rock ledges — pure carefree joy.

We had halo-halo that evening — a towering Filipino shaved-ice dessert served inside a coconut shell, loaded with sweet beans, nata de coco, kaong, purple ube ice cream, corn flakes, and coloured jellies. Sweet, creamy, chaotic, and magnificent. Jeremy ate two.

Halo-halo in a coconut shell — ube ice cream, jellies, corn flakes, and shaved ice. The Philippines in a bowl.

Then came the sunset. It is not an exaggeration to say it was one of the most beautiful things I have ever seen. The sun dropped between banks of orange cloud and sank perfectly into the Sibuyan Sea, its light turning everything copper and rose. Paraw sailboats — traditional outrigger sailing boats with vivid blue and red canvas — moved slowly across the burning horizon, their silhouettes impossibly elegant. Two figures in the shallows worked a stand-up paddleboard. Nobody spoke.

The Boracay sunset — paraw sailboats on a burning horizon. The most beautiful 15 minutes of the whole trip.

The next morning was grey and moody, the sea a deep teal green, a lone swimmer visible far out, a paraw on the edge of the storm. A different kind of beautiful — quieter, more serious — but beautiful all the same.

Boracay the morning after — overcast, the sea deep teal, the beach almost empty.

Someone was still carving the next day's sand sculpture as we packed to leave. Love Boracay. Nov 26 2023. A young man bent over his work with a trowel, completely absorbed, creating something beautiful that would be gone by morning.

"Love Boracay — Nov 26 2023" — the daily sand sculpture, completed and perfect.
The sculptor at work, mixing the binding compound that holds the sand together overnight.

"He carves a masterpiece every day, knowing the tide will take it. That, I thought, is how to live — give everything, hold nothing back, and let the ocean do the rest."

Epilogue

27 Days, One Archipelago, and a Friendship Deepened

23 November 2023  ·  Homeward

We flew out of Cebu on 23 November — Jeremy to Jakarta, me connecting home — bags heavier with gifts, phones full of photographs, heads full of moments that would take months to fully process.

The Philippines gave us everything we had hoped for and much more. It gave us the earthy coolness of Baguio's pine forest and the dizzying height of a volcanic ridge at sunrise. It gave us the ancient walls of Intramuros and the roaring chaos of the Manila jeepney. It gave us tuna bigger than either of us, lechon crackling under a glass counter, and halo-halo that tasted like a reward. It gave us Alona Beach's sandcastles, the Cathedral limestone arches of El Nido, the serene Buddhas of Coron, and the most beautiful sunset I have ever seen, framed by paraw sails against a burning Boracay sky.

Most of all, it gave Jeremy and me twenty-seven days of laughing, arguing about the map, getting the wrong jeepney, sweating on mountain trails, ordering with our fingers and hoping for the best, and being welcomed everywhere with a warmth that the Philippines seems to generate naturally, like sunlight.

We are already talking about coming back.

The plan that started it all — 9 destinations, 27 days, one unforgettable friendship journey.
