S02: The Day I Lost My iPhone in Portugal

How did a spare phone, a local SIM card, and two helpful Portuguese police officers turn a disaster into a story worth telling?一部备用手机、一张本地SIM卡，还有两位热心的葡萄牙警察，如何让一场灾难变成了值得讲述的故事？

P
Paul
Albufeira → Beja, Portugal · 23 June 2025 · Estimated reading time: 5 minutes

On 23 June 2025, I was travelling solo in Portugal. At seventy, I still prefer hostels and public buses to package tours — they keep the trip honest and the wallet light.

That afternoon, I boarded a Rede Expressos bus in Albufeira. It was the 14:45 service to Beja, scheduled to arrive at 16:15 — just a short ride through the Portuguese countryside.

I waited at the terminal with my small bag, watching other travellers come and go. Somewhere along the way, the bus pulled into a roadside stop for a short break.

I rushed to the toilet, then bought myself an ice cream from a small stall nearby. There was a wooden table close to the bus, in the shade. I sat down and started enjoying my ice cream — the kind of small, simple pleasure that makes solo travel feel good at seventy.

I had put my iPhone on the table next to me.

When the driver called everyone back, I stood up in a hurry, finished the last bite of my ice cream, and climbed onto the bus. I did not look back at the table.

The Moment I Realised发现手机丢失的那一刻

The bus had already pulled onto the highway when my hand reached into my pocket and found nothing. I checked the other pocket. My bag. The seat beside me. Nothing.

My iPhone was gone.

I felt my chest tighten. This was my new iPhone. All my travel photos, my booking confirmations, my contacts, my translation app, my maps — everything I needed to keep travelling was in that phone. And we were already on the highway, moving fast. There was no way for the driver to stop. No way to turn around.

For a few minutes, I just sat there feeling sick. At seventy, you learn that panic does not help anything. But knowing that does not always stop the panic from coming.

Thinking Clearly Again重新冷静下来

I took a few deep breaths and reminded myself:

I am not helpless. I have options.

I always travel with a spare phone in my bag — an old one I keep for emergencies, exactly for situations like this. I pulled it out and started thinking.

Step one: I needed a local SIM card so I could use mobile data on the spare phone. After arriving in Beja, I found a small shop and bought one.

Step two: I opened the Find My iPhone app and signed in.

And there it was. A small green dot on the map.

The address was clear: Praceta Jaime Cortesão 5, 7800-028 Beja. Someone had picked up my iPhone, brought it onto the same bus, and taken it home to their flat in Beja.

I had been so close. They probably walked past me at the station.

Why the Thief Could Not Lock Me Out为什么小偷无法把我锁在外面

Here is something I learned that day, and I want every traveller to know it:

My new iPhone uses an eSIM, not a physical SIM card.

When a thief picks up a stolen iPhone, the first thing they usually do is remove the SIM card so the owner cannot track it. But an eSIM is built into the phone itself. It cannot be popped out with a pin. The person who took my phone had no idea how to disable it, and he could not figure out how to log in either — my phone was locked with a passcode and Face ID.

So my phone kept reporting its location. Quietly. Honestly. From inside someone else's flat.

I will never go back to a physical SIM after this.

Straight to the Police直接去找警察

I had booked a hostel in Beja for the night. As soon as I checked in and dropped my bag near my bunk, I did not even sit down.

I walked back out and took a taxi straight to the nearest police station. I showed them the map on my spare phone — with the exact address. The officer looked at the green dot, nodded, and made a phone call.

The next day, officers went to the flat at Praceta Jaime Cortesão 5. They knocked on the door. They asked the person inside whether he had picked up a phone at the rest stop on the Albufeira–Beja route. He admitted it.

That same day — the day after I had lost it — I had my iPhone back in my hand.

The streets of Beja, when I walked them again, looked different. Friendlier. Lighter.

I do not know exactly what the police said to the man, and I did not ask. What mattered was that the system worked. A spare phone, a local SIM, the Find My app, and helpful Portuguese police officers had turned a disaster into a story.

What I Learned我学到的教训

I want to share the lessons in plain words, because at our age these things matter even more:

  1. Carry a spare phone. It does not need to be fancy. Even an old one you no longer use is enough. It is your insurance.
  2. Use an eSIM on your main phone. A thief cannot rip out what is not physically there.
  3. Always have Find My iPhone turned on before you travel. Check it before you leave home.
  4. Buy a phone lanyard or string. This is what I changed straight after this trip. Now my iPhone is tied to me with a strong string, kept around my neck or attached to my bag. It cannot be left behind on a table anymore.
  5. Know where the local police station is. In most countries I have visited, the police are kind and helpful to older tourists. Do not be afraid to ask.
  6. Slow down at rest stops. This was my real mistake. I was rushing. I was excited about the ice cream. I forgot to do my "table check" before standing up — wallet, phone, glasses, bag. Now I always do it.

Looking Back回头想想

With my iPhone back in my pocket, I could finally enjoy the rest of the trip. I went out on a small boat ride along the Algarve coast — the kind of thing I had been looking forward to for months. The yellow sandstone cliffs. The arches. The sea caves.

I took these photos with the same iPhone that, only a day before, I had thought was gone forever.

Sitting on that little boat, the water gently rocking under me, I thought to myself: I am seventy years old, alone in a foreign country, and I solved this myself. That feeling was worth more than the phone.

Travel keeps me young because it keeps reminding me that I can still figure things out. The ice cream was good too.
