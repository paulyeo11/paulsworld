Into the Green

A morning hike at the Reservoir — through jungle, ancient roots, and water the colour of jade. Singapore, 16 June 2026.

The Beginning

Before the Light Found the Trail, I Was Already There

16 June 2026  ·  8:49 AM  ·  Reservoir entrance

The barrier was down and the overcast sky sat heavy above the treeline. Not yet eight fifty in the morning, and the world still had that quiet, half-awake quality — the city somewhere behind me, the jungle directly ahead. I stood at the entrance gate and breathed it in. Damp earth, green things, the faint mineral smell of water not far away. This is why I come.

I am seventy years old. Retired at sixty-seven. Some mornings I open my laptop first and the world crowds in before I have even had breakfast. This morning I did it the right way: laced up my shoes, walked to the trailhead, and let the jungle be my first conversation of the day.

8:49 AM — the barrier at the entrance. Overcast sky, cool air, the trail waiting beyond.

"There is a particular kind of stillness at a trailhead in the morning. The jungle is already awake. You are just catching up."

Into the Forest

Mud, Roots, and the Smell of Something Ancient

The trail in  ·  Dense canopy overhead

The path turned muddy within the first few minutes — proper jungle mud, the kind that grabs your shoe and holds it for a moment, just to remind you who is in charge out here. I did not mind. Mud means real forest. Mud means the trail does not get crowds.

What stopped me almost immediately were the roots. Not small, tidy roots at the edge of the path — these were enormous, erupting out of the ground in great twisted arches, the base of trees so old and so large that their root systems had taken on a life entirely their own. I put my hand on one and it was hard as stone, warm from the morning air, covered in a thin bloom of moss. These trees had been here long before I was born and they would be here long after. Standing next to something like that does something to your sense of scale.

The trail — real jungle, real mud, the canopy closing in overhead from the very first steps.
Ancient roots — these trees have been here for generations.
The canopy closes above — green all the way up.

The canopy above was so thick that the morning sky had all but disappeared. Light came down in fragments, moving slowly as the leaves shifted in a breeze too faint to feel at ground level. The whole effect was of being inside something — not trapped, not confined, but held. Contained by green in a way that felt like care rather than claustrophobia.

Strangler vines — nature doing what it does, slowly and completely.
Tall, straight, and utterly indifferent — the forest goes on without us.
✦ ✦ ✦
Encounters

A Butterfly, Some Vines, and a Sign That Said No

Deep in the trail  ·  The forest shares its secrets

I had only been walking for about fifteen minutes when a brown butterfly settled on a leaf just off the path. It sat perfectly still, wings slightly open, as if posing — or perhaps just warming itself in the thin morning light. I stopped and watched it for a long moment. Out here, these are the moments that do not come with any appointment. You just have to be present enough to notice them.

A brown butterfly resting on a leaf — the jungle's quiet gift to the patient walker.

The vines here were something else. Great twisted ropes of vegetation looping from tree to tree, some as thick as my arm, others thin as wire, all of them working their slow patient way toward the light. This is what the jungle does in the background while we are all busy with our lives — it grows, wraps, consumes, transforms. It is an entire economy of energy, invisible to most people because it moves too slowly for a screen to capture.

Red earth and steep ground — this is not a flat path.
Deeper in — the trail narrows and the world shrinks to what is immediately around you.

And then I came around a bend and there it was: a yellow NO ENTRY sign, planted at the edge of a side path that disappeared into the green. It looked entirely serious. I stood in front of it for a moment, noted it carefully, and continued on my way down the main trail. I am seventy years old. I know when the jungle is speaking and when a sign is.

A NO ENTRY sign deep in the forest — some paths are not for walking.
✦ ✦ ✦
First Light on Water

Green Water Through the Trees, Around 9:25

9:25 AM  ·  The reservoir reveals itself

I had been walking for about thirty-five minutes when I first caught a glimpse of it — through a gap in the trees, a flash of green that was not the jungle. Water. The reservoir, showing itself for the first time between the trunks and the hanging vines. That particular shade of green is not something you can quite describe until you have seen it: deep, still, slightly luminous, the colour of a polished jade stone or the heart of a dark emerald.

I stopped and looked. The water was barely visible — just a hint of it through the canopy — but it changed the whole feel of the walk. You always hike differently when you know the water is close.

First glimpse — jade-green water through a gap in the trees.
NParks trail markers — the park has been carefully tagged and maintained.

I passed a cluster of NParks tree tags fastened to the trunks along the path — small metal plates, each one identifying a tree by name. Someone had walked this same trail before me and stopped at each of these trees to record them, name them, make them part of a living inventory. I liked that enormously. The jungle here is not wild in the sense of being unknown — it is known, named, and cared for. Singapore's relationship with its green spaces is one of the things I am quietly proud of.

The trail pushes on — and the water is getting closer.
✦ ✦ ✦
The Water's Edge

Jade Green and Perfectly Still

At the reservoir  ·  The reward for the walk in

And then the trees opened and I was standing at the edge of the reservoir, and all the effort of the trail vanished in an instant.

The water was extraordinary. That shade of green — deep, calm, completely still — with the surrounding jungle reflected in it so perfectly that for a moment you could not tell where the real trees ended and the mirrored ones began. The sky above was still overcast, which meant there was no glare, no harsh light — just this pure, clean, luminous green stretching out before me. It was one of the most beautiful things I have seen in Singapore.

The reservoir at last — jade-green water, jungle reflections, absolute stillness.

"The water was the colour of jade — deep, still, and perfectly clear. The jungle reflected in it so truly that the world seemed to have doubled itself, the trees growing both up and down."

The reflection is almost perfect — jungle above, jungle below.
Looking out — Singapore's drinking water, held in the forest.

I walked slowly along the edge. The water was glassy with just the faintest movement at the surface — the kind of trembling that suggests depth rather than motion. A blue maintenance vessel was working somewhere in the middle distance, a small purposeful shape in all that green space. It was a useful reminder: this beautiful, wild-feeling place is also Singapore's drinking water supply. Every drop of that jade water eventually comes out of a tap. The forest around it exists, in part, to protect it. I found that thought moving rather than industrial — nature and utility working together in the same quiet place.

A blue maintenance vessel working on the reservoir — a reminder that this is Singapore's drinking water supply.

What the Reservoir Showed Me

Fungi, Buds, Lichen — the Forest Floor's Secret Life

At the water  ·  Looking closely

I have learned, over many years of walking in nature, that the reward for slowing down is always greater than the reward for covering ground. Standing at the reservoir's edge with no schedule and nowhere to be, I started looking more carefully. And the more carefully I looked, the more the forest gave me.

Bracket fungi on a half-submerged log — shelf upon shelf of rust and cream, growing at the waterline.
Small pink buds on a branch hanging over the water — something about to bloom.

A fallen log lay half in and half out of the water at the reservoir's edge, and on its exposed side, bracket fungi were growing in perfect shelves — rust-coloured on top, cream underneath, stacked one above the other like the floors of a tiny building. They were extraordinary up close. Completely at home at the waterline, doing exactly what fungi have been doing in places like this for millions of years.

White mushrooms on the forest floor — delicate, precise, completely certain of themselves.
Lichen on old wood — slow, patient, and absolutely magnificent at close range.

Nearby, a branch hung out over the water carrying small pink buds — not quite open yet, waiting for something, perhaps a few more degrees of warmth or the right moment in the morning. I stood underneath them and looked up and thought: this is what the jungle looks like when nobody is watching. Then it occurred to me — it always looks like this. I am the visitor. The buds do not perform for anyone.

On the forest floor nearby, a cluster of white mushrooms had pushed up through the leaf litter — bright, clean, almost impossibly tidy against the dark soil. And on the old logs further in, great patches of lichen spread in grey-green blooms, soft and dry to the touch, looking from a distance like something painted on. Up close they are one of the most complex living things in a forest, and one of the most overlooked. I am trying to overlook less.

More from the water's edge — moments worth slowing down for.

The Walk Back

Everything Looks Different on the Way Out

10:00 AM onwards  ·  Back through the jungle

The walk back through the jungle has a different quality to the walk in. Going in, everything is unknown — your eyes are scanning for the trail, checking for tree roots, wondering what is around the next bend. Coming back out, you have already been inside. The forest is familiar. And so you see it differently.

I noticed things on the return I had walked past on the way in — a particular arrangement of roots where three trees had grown so close together their bases had merged into a single knot of wood; a patch of ferns so bright they seemed to be generating their own light; a bird I never quite saw but heard clearly, a two-note call that followed me for about five minutes before I turned a corner and lost it.

The trail on the way out — familiar now, but still surprising.
The forest floor — things I walked past on the way in, noticed on the way out.

The forest going outward — same trees, different light, different eyes.

10:27 AM

Out of the Jungle, Changed by It

10:27 AM  ·  End of hike  ·  1.5 hours in the forest

I stepped back out onto the path near the entrance barrier at ten twenty-seven. One hour and thirty-eight minutes in the jungle. My shoes were muddy, my shirt was damp, and I was holding my phone full of photographs of fungi and butterflies and green water. I felt excellent.

There is something the jungle does that no amount of exercise in a gym or walking on a treadmill can replicate. It is not just the physical — legs worked, lungs opened, body moved. It is everything else: the smell, the light, the silence that is not quite silence because it is full of insect sound and bird calls and the creak of branches. The sense, walking back out, that you have been somewhere that does not need you — and that is exactly why it is so good for you.

Nearly out — the light changes as the jungle opens up ahead.
One last look back. The jungle holds no dramatic farewell. It simply continues.
10:27 AM — back at the barrier. An hour and thirty-eight minutes well spent.

"The jungle does not need you. It was here before you arrived and it will be here after you leave. That is precisely why spending time inside it does something to your sense of proportion that nothing else quite manages."

What I Took Away from This Morning

  • Go early. Before eight-thirty if you can. The light is different, the trail is quiet, and the air has not yet been claimed by the day.
  • Slow down at the water. The reservoir is the reward. Do not walk past it — stand at the edge and actually look. It is worth five minutes of stillness.
  • Look low. The drama in a jungle is not always up in the canopy. Fungi, mushrooms, lichen, root systems — the ground level rewards the patient observer.
  • Leave the earphones out. The jungle has its own soundtrack. It is better than anything you have queued.
  • This is Singapore's water. That jade-green reservoir is not decoration. Every time you stand at its edge, you are standing next to the city's lifeblood. It is worth a moment of respect.

I will come back. I always do. The reservoir will be there, the mud will be there, the butterflies will land on whatever leaf they feel like landing on, and the bracket fungi will keep growing their perfect rust-coloured shelves at the waterline without the slightest interest in my opinions about them. That is the whole point.
