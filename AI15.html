AI15: My First Mandarin Speech at Toastmasters — Sharing AI at 70

How a retired adventurer stood on stage and spoke about artificial intelligence — in Mandarin

By Paul Yeo  ·  June 2026  ·  AI Journey Series

On 26 June 2026, I stood in front of a room full of people at my Toastmasters club and delivered my first Mandarin speech — about my one month of living with AI. I am 70 years old. I was nervous. And it was one of the most memorable things I have ever done.

I have given speeches before. But this one was different. The topic was technical — artificial intelligence — and I had to explain it clearly and personally in Mandarin. Not just read a script, but connect with the audience. That is the Toastmasters way.

Here is the story of how it came together.

· · ·

The Challenge挑战

Speaking in Mandarin about AI to a live audience at 70 is not a small thing. My Mandarin is decent — but technical vocabulary in any language is hard. Words like "artificial intelligence", "portfolio analysis", "cognitive decline" — these need to land naturally, not sound like a textbook.

On top of that, Toastmasters speeches have a structure. You need an opening that hooks the audience, clear sections, a key message, and a strong close. You cannot just wander. You need a plan — and you need to remember it.

I also had a personal challenge: I have never been a person who memorises things easily. I was not going to read from paper. That is not how I do things.

The hardest part was not speaking in Mandarin. It was trusting that I would remember what to say when I was standing up there alone.

— My honest fear before the speech
· · ·

What I Shared我分享了什么

My speech title was "我在过去一个月的人工智能体验" — My One Month of AI Experience. Here is what I told the audience, in plain English:

Two years ago, I tried to learn AI and failed. The technology was not ready. My vision was simple — I wanted an AI to help me plan travel. It did not work. I gave up.

Then, one month ago, I tried again. This time I subscribed to Claude AI for $20 a month. Everything changed.

My working style became: work one hour, rest four hours. The AI does so much that I simply do not need to grind. An hour with Claude can accomplish what used to take a full day.

I told the audience what AI has done for me in just one month:

  • Travel planning — I used AI to plan my Xinjiang self-drive trip (north and south). It told me that hotels in Kanas Lake need to be booked months in advance. That single tip alone saved the trip.
  • Writing an eBook — I turned my travel experiences into a full eBook. Without AI, this dream would never have happened.
  • Stock and options analysis — I feed my entire IBKR and Tiger portfolio into AI and get instant analysis. Like having a private financial advisor on call, 24 hours a day.
  • Writing and editing — When my articles feel messy, I ask AI to help me restructure them. It does not rewrite my voice — it sharpens it.
  • Saving time — What used to take four hours of searching, AI does in minutes.

But I also shared the danger. If too many people rely on AI to think for them, we will see a generation that cannot think for themselves. Just like 80% of drivers who use GPS today — they get completely lost without it. The map-reading skill is gone.

· · ·

The Memory Trick记忆技巧

Here is how I remembered the speech without notes. I did not memorise word for word. Instead, I used body-part memory anchors — a technique David (my AI assistant) helped me build.

The idea is simple: attach each section of the speech to a body part, in order from top to bottom. Your body becomes your outline. When you are on stage and your mind goes blank, you just run down your own body — hair, forehead, eyes, nose, mouth… and each one triggers the right section.

I ran through those ten anchors in my head before I stood up. On stage, I never needed notes. Each body part pulled the next section right out of memory. David built that system with me — and it worked perfectly.

· · ·

The Key Message核心信息

Everything in my speech pointed to one sentence. I saved it for last. I looked at the audience and said it slowly, in Mandarin:

使用AI来帮助你，但不要让AI来替代你的思考。

— Use AI to help you, but never let AI replace your thinking.

That is the whole philosophy. AI is a tool — an extraordinary, powerful, almost magical tool. But a tool has no wisdom. Wisdom comes from you. The moment you stop thinking and just accept what AI tells you, you have handed over the one thing that makes you human.

I told them: even the content of this speech — I remembered it with AI's help. But the story is mine. The experience is mine. The words came from my life. AI organised it. I delivered it.

The Standing Ovation Moment

At 70, I built a website, planned a Xinjiang road trip, analysed my stock portfolio, wrote an eBook, and delivered a Mandarin speech at Toastmasters — all in one month. Every single one of those things was done with AI, not by AI. That is the difference that matters.

· · ·

Still Learning, Still Adventuring

People sometimes look at me and assume retirement means slowing down. I retired at 67. I am now 70. In the past month alone I have done more new things than in many years before AI came into my life.

The speech on 26 June was proof — not just that AI works, but that it is never too late to learn something that changes everything. Standing on that stage in front of my Toastmasters club, speaking in Mandarin about artificial intelligence, at 70 years old — that is an adventure too.

And the best part? I remembered every word.
