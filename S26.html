Same Reservoir. Different Door.

On Day 2, I came in through the secret BKE side — squeezing through a gap in the fence, running from a recovery car, earning every step the hard way. It was dramatic, it was memorable, and it got me to Upper Peirce Reservoir at 9 AM with the whole water to myself.

This morning — Day 3 — I took the other approach entirely.

I started from the PUB water plant. The official entrance. The organised, proper, nobody-is-going-to-radio-for-backup entry point. And then I turned left.

That left turn made all the difference.

"Three mornings, three routes, the same reservoir. Each one showed me something the others could not. That is what daily hiking teaches you — that familiar places have more sides than you think."

The PUB Gate and What Lies Beyond It

The PUB water plant sits quietly at the edge of the reservoir. Most people walk past it on their way to the more popular trail entrances — the ones that appear on hiking apps and have proper signage and crowds on weekends. I have walked past it myself, on previous mornings, thinking: I will try that route another day.

Today was that day.

I arrived at the gate at 8 AM. The air was cool and heavy with that particular Singapore morning smell — warm earth, wet leaves, the faint chemical-clean scent of treated water somewhere behind the fence. The reservoir was already visible through the trees, catching the first real light of the day.

I turned left.

The Side Nobody Talks About

The left path follows the reservoir boundary in a direction most hikers do not take. Narrower. Less worn. A strip of earth and fallen leaves pressed between the PUB security fence on one side and a wall of jungle on the other. Within two minutes, I had the route entirely to myself.

Then — a blocked drainage channel. Rocks and debris piled across the path, jungle growing right over it from both sides. No way through.

I looked further along and found a large iron grate covering a deep drain structure — thick rusted bars, water pumps and heavy pipes visible below. The engineering that keeps Singapore's reservoirs running, right here at eye level. No way through that either.

This is the moment every solo hiker knows. The planned path is not the available path. You stop. You look around. And you find another way in.

I walked further along and found it — the official NParks trail entrance. White bollards, a cluster of signs: Hiking Only, No Biking, No Pets Allowed. And one that made me smile: What To Do When You Encounter Wild Boars. On Day 2 I slipped through a secret gap in the BKE fence. Today I walked through the proper front door. Both felt like a victory.

"Turning left when everyone else turns right — that is where the good things are. On a hiking trail, and in life."

New Views of Old Water

What surprised me most was how different the reservoir looked from this side. On Day 2, coming through the BKE jungle, the water had felt like a reward — something earned through difficulty, revealed at the end of a hard walk. On Day 1, at Chestnut, the reservoir was a quiet moment in the middle of a loop.

Today, from the left-turn path, the reservoir felt wide. Expansive. Like I was seeing the whole of it for the first time — not just one bank, but the full curve of the water from edge to edge.

The morning light was different too. Day 2 I was at the reservoir by 9:10 AM, and the sun was already high enough to flatten the light. Today, arriving from this direction, the angle was lower — the water was lit from the side, turning it into something between gold and green, the trees casting long reflections across the surface.

I sat for a while. I ate my orange. Nobody came.

What Three Days Taught Me

Here is what I know now that I did not know on Day 1:

The same reservoir is not the same reservoir twice. You can go back to the same water, the same jungle, the same Singapore morning — and find a completely different walk, depending on which door you choose and which direction you turn.

Day 1 was a loop. Day 2 was an expedition. Day 3 was an exploration. The same general area, three completely different experiences. Three completely different things I carried home with me.

"Retirement is not about running out of new places to go. It is about discovering that the places you already know have more corners than you have seen."

Why I Keep Coming Back

People sometimes ask me why I hike the same parks and the same reservoirs instead of going somewhere new each time. This week answers that question. Three days at the same reservoir — and each morning was entirely its own thing.

A new country gives you novelty. A familiar trail gives you depth. Both matter. But at seventy, hiking three mornings in a row at a reservoir five minutes from home, I think I understand something about depth that I did not fully understand before retirement.

You have to go back. You have to look at the same thing again, from a slightly different angle. That is when it gives you something new.

Three Days Complete

I walked out the same way I walked in — past the PUB gate, back onto the road, back toward home. My legs were warm, my steps were lighter than when I started, and my phone said I had been walking for two hours.

Three days. Three routes. Three different versions of Upper Peirce Reservoir in the morning.

When I got home, I showered and thought about the week. On Tuesday I did Chestnut. On Wednesday the BKE. On Thursday the waterworks path. Each one under three hours. Each one finished before the day had properly started. Each one the kind of morning that makes the rest of the day feel like a bonus.

That is what retirement looks like when you refuse to let it go still.

"I am 70 years old. I have walked this reservoir from three different directions this week. Every morning, just me, the water, and one orange. If you told my 40-year-old self this would be the highlight of his Thursday, he would not have believed you. He would have been wrong."
