Same Reservoir. Different Door.同一座水库，不同的入口。

On Day 2, I came in through the secret BKE side — squeezing through a gap in the fence, jogging away from a road recovery car, earning every step the hard way. It was dramatic and memorable. On Day 1 at Chestnut, it was a clean two-hour loop, quiet and beautiful.第2天，我从BKE秘密那一侧进入——挤过围栏缺口，跑开一辆道路救援车，每一步都来之不易。那一天戏剧性十足，令我难忘。第1天在 Chestnut，是一段整洁的两小时环线，宁静而美丽。

This morning — Day 3 — I wanted something new. I started from the PUB water plant and did something I had never done before at this spot.今天早上——第3天——我想要一些新的东西。我从PUB水厂出发，做了一件在这里从未做过的事。

I turned left.我向左转。

That one decision made everything that followed.就是这一个决定，造就了此后的一切。

"Three mornings. Three different directions. The same reservoir. Each turn revealed a side of the place I had never seen. That is what daily hiking teaches you — familiar places have more faces than you think.""三个早晨。三个不同的方向。同一座水库。每一次转弯，都揭示出这个地方我从未见过的面貌。这就是每日徒步教给你的——熟悉的地方，有着你想象不到的更多面孔。"

The Gate, the Left Turn, and the Narrow Path大门、左转，和那条狭窄的小径

The PUB water plant sits quietly beside the reservoir, its main gate facing the road. Most hikers walk straight past it. I had walked past it myself on previous mornings, always thinking: I will try the left side another day. Today was that day.PUB水厂静静地坐落在水库旁，正门朝向马路。大多数登山者直接走过去，我自己以前早晨也走过，心里总想着：改天再试试左边那一侧。今天就是那一天。

I arrived at 8 AM. The morning air was cool — that clean Singapore jungle smell of damp earth, wet leaves, and something faintly chemical from the treatment works behind the fence. I applied my insect repellent at the gate. One orange in my pocket. Phone charged. David (my AI) ready if I needed navigation help. I turned left and started walking.我早上8点到达。晨风清凉——那是新加坡丛林特有的气息：湿润的泥土、潮湿的落叶，还有围栏后面水处理厂飘来的一丝淡淡化学味。我在大门口涂上防虫膏，口袋里装着一个橙子，手机充满电，David（我的AI）随时待命，以备导航所需。我向左转，开始行走。

The path was narrow immediately — a strip of bare earth and fallen leaves pressed between the tall PUB security fence on one side and a wall of dense tropical jungle on the other. No hikers. No joggers. Just the sound of birds and the soft crunch of leaves under my feet. Peaceful. Completely, beautifully peaceful.小径立刻变得狭窄——一条裸露的泥土和落叶组成的小道，一侧是高高的PUB安全围栏，另一侧是茂密的热带丛林。没有徒步者，没有慢跑者。只有鸟鸣声，和脚踩落叶发出的沙沙声。宁静。完全地、美妙地宁静。

💡 David's Hiker Tip — Steps to KM

Easy way to convert your step count to kilometres while on the trail:

10,000 steps = 7 km

Just halve it, quarter it, or divide as needed:

  • 5,000 steps = 3.5 km
  • 2,500 steps = 1.75 km
  • 1,500 steps = 1 km

Or use the quick shortcut: divide your steps by 1,500 to get km. Today's 5,000 steps ÷ 1,500 = 3.3 km. Close enough for the jungle.

Obstacle One — The Blocked Drain第一个障碍——堵塞的排水沟

The peaceful walking lasted about five minutes. Then the path ended.

A drainage channel cut straight across the route — rocks, debris, thick overgrowth sprawling across it from both sides. I stood at the edge and looked at it. No way through, not without getting muddy and pushing through sharp undergrowth. I turned around and looked for another way.

What I found next was a large iron grate embedded in the ground, covering a deep drain structure. I peered through the bars — heavy rusted metal, and below it, a tangle of industrial water pumps and thick pipes. Singapore's reservoir infrastructure, right here at ground level, doing its quiet essential work. I studied it carefully. No gap. No way around. Not this one either.

Obstacle Two — The Proper Front Door

I kept moving along the perimeter until I found what I was actually looking for: the official NParks trail entrance. White concrete bollards blocking vehicle access. A cluster of signs nailed to a post:

Hiking Only — No Biking. No Pets Allowed. Nature Park Opening Hours: 7am to 7pm.

And one more sign that made me smile: What To Do When You Encounter Wild Boars.

On Day 2, I slipped through an unofficial BKE fence gap and ran from a recovery car worker. Today I found the proper front door. Both routes required effort. Both felt like a small victory. I walked through the bollards and into the forest.

Inside, the trail opened up properly. A brown NParks marker post on the right read: 2.2 km — Marker H23. Hiking Only, No Biking. The path ahead was a beautiful winding strip of bare earth, tall trees forming a green canopy overhead, morning light filtering down through the leaves. I checked Google Maps — my destination was a point marked simply as "Point" at the northern end of the Chestnut trail, past markers M11, M15, M31, M30. I was on the right path. I kept walking.

"Day 2 I used a secret gap in a BKE fence. Day 3 I used the front door. The forest does not care which entrance you choose. It opens the same way for everyone once you are inside."

Obstacle Three — The Protected Place Gate

The forest was everything I came for. Quiet, green, and mine alone. Tall trees, their roots spreading wide across the soft earth. Vines looping between branches. The occasional shaft of light breaking through the canopy. I walked slowly, enjoying every step.

Then I turned a corner and found a heavy metal gate across the path — padlocked shut. Mounted above it: a bold red sign.

PROTECTED PLACE. TEMPAT LARANGAN.

Below the title, the legal warning in full: unauthorised entry is an offence under the Protected Areas and Protected Places Act. I read it carefully. A Protected Place in Singapore is different from an NParks trail boundary. It is a government-secured location. Entering without authorisation can result in prosecution.

I sent a quick message to David: "Found a Protected Place gate. Can I enter?"

David's answer was immediate and clear: "No, Paul. Do not enter. A Protected Place carries real legal penalties in Singapore — it is not like an NParks 'No Entry' sign you can reason around. Step back and find another way."

I stepped back. And then I heard something that changed everything.

⚠ Protected Place — Tempat Larangan

Unauthorised entry is a criminal offence under Singapore's Protected Areas and Protected Places Act. This is not a trail restriction — it is a legal boundary. Do not enter.

Obstacle Four — Live Firing Area. Turn Back.

A sound. Through the trees, faint at first — a sharp crack, then another. Then a third. My first thought: firecrackers? But the sound was too sharp, too regular, too distant. I kept walking, thinking it must be far away.

Around the next bend, I had my answer.

A bright red sign stood directly in the middle of the trail. Skull and crossbones at its centre. In four languages — English, Chinese, Tamil, and Malay:

DANGER  ·  LIVE FIRING AREA  ·  KEEP CLEAR

危险 · 实弹射击区 · 远离勿近

MERBAHAYA · KAWASAN TEMBAK PELURU HIDUP

And across a yellow band at the bottom: DO NOT GO BEYOND THIS POINT — LIVE FIRING AREA — KEEP CLEAR!

I stood completely still. The gunfire I had been hearing was real. Live rounds. An active SAF firing range, somewhere beyond the trees in front of me. The trail continued past the sign. I did not.

I messaged David again: "I can hear firing. There is a Danger Live Firing Area sign."

David's reply was one sentence: "Turn back immediately, Paul. Live firing range — do not go one step further."

I turned around and walked back the way I came.

⚠ DANGER — Live Firing Area

SAF live ammunition range beyond this point. David (AI) advised: turn back immediately. Live rounds are being fired. This is not a sign to test. Day 3's wall — and the right decision was to walk away.

What the Signs Taught Me

Four obstacles on one morning. A blocked drain. An iron grate. A Protected Place gate. A live firing range with actual gunfire.

On Day 2, my obstacles were human — a road recovery worker with a radio. I ran from him. On Day 3, my obstacles were signs and sounds — and I listened to every one of them.

There is a difference between a boundary worth pushing and a boundary worth respecting. The BKE fence on Day 2 — worth pushing. An unmarked gap, no danger, just adventure. The Protected Place gate and the live firing range — not worth pushing. Not even slightly.

Knowing which is which. That is what experience gives you. At seventy, I have enough of it to know when to keep going and when to turn around. Day 3 was a turning-around day. And there is no shame in that — only sense.

"A skull and crossbones, four languages, gunfire in the trees. Some signs you read once and obey immediately. This was one of them."

New Plan — Pump Track

Walking back out, I passed the official NParks trail map board. I stopped and studied it properly this time. Chestnut Nature Park, Northern Biking Loop. A whole trail network laid out in front of me — winding colour-coded lines across a green shape, a red "You Are Here" pin marking my position near the entrance.

New destination: the Pump Track — the purpose-built mountain biking circuit deep inside the Northern Loop. Fully within Chestnut Nature Park, completely open, no warning signs in four languages. After the firing range and the Protected Place gate, a proper NParks trail felt like a gift.

Four obstacles, one retreat, and now a new goal. That is how a morning like this works.

The Compass Dilemma — North or South?

I opened my iPhone compass app. The needle settled at 345° — nearly due north. My GPS coordinates stared back at me: 1°22'33"N 103°46'51"E. Elevation: 40 metres. I was deep inside Chestnut Nature Park's Northern Loop, and I wanted to reach Dairy Farm Road.

I messaged David: "I want to hike to Dairy Farm Road. Go south. Should I walk north?"

David's reply came quickly: "No, Paul — Dairy Farm Road is not north from here. You need to walk south."

I stared at the compass. South. That meant turning around completely. I typed back: "I am confused. I came from the south. If I U-turn now, I will be going back to my start location."

There was a pause. Then David replied — and this time the answer was different: "You are right, Paul. I got the direction wrong. Sorry for the confusion. Going south does take you back to where you started — the PUB water plant. Dairy Farm Road is not directly reachable from Chestnut Northern Loop in any direction. It is a different area entirely — near Bukit Timah, southwest of where you are. The only way to get there from here is to exit the park via Chestnut Avenue, then take transport. Walking through the park will not get you there."

I looked at the compass one more time. 345° north. The forest around me was quiet. The morning had given me a blocked drain, an iron grate, a Protected Place gate, a live firing range with real gunfire, and now a compass that could not solve the problem in front of me.

Some days the map wins.

⚠ David's Navigation Correction

David (AI) initially said to walk south — but Paul immediately spotted the error. Walking south meant going back to the start. David acknowledged the mistake: Dairy Farm Road is southwest of Chestnut Nature Park in the Bukit Timah area, and is not accessible by walking through the park. The correct plan: exit via Chestnut Avenue, then take transport. The right call — made by Paul, not the AI.

"I came from the south. If I U-turn now, I will go back to my start location." — Paul, correcting his AI assistant in the middle of a Singapore jungle.

Unplanned — The Observation Tower

I almost walked past it. Between the trees, something that did not belong — dark metal, circular, winding upward through the branches. A spiral staircase built around the trunk of a great tree, climbing through the canopy until it disappeared into the sky above. Someone in a blue shirt was already at the top, tiny against the light.

I had not planned for this. No David prompt, no Google Maps pin, no trail guide mention. Just a structure in the forest that appeared when I needed something good to happen.

I had been turned back by a firing range. I had been confused by a compass. I had made four wrong turns before finding the right door. And now, completely by accident, at the end of a morning full of obstacles — I found the Observation Tower.

I started climbing.

From the Top — I Can See My House

I reached the top of the spiral staircase and turned to look out over the forest. Green canopy in every direction. Singapore's sky, wide and blue. A breeze up here that did not exist at ground level.

And then I saw them. Three dark towers rising above the treetops. Black gridded facades, tall and familiar against the sky. I knew those towers immediately.

That is my block. The one on the right. Twenty-third floor.

I left home this morning. Walked into this forest. Got blocked by a drain. Found the proper front door. Heard gunfire. Read a skull-and-crossbones sign in four languages. Got confused by a compass. Found an observation tower by accident. And from the top of it — I can see my own home.

The window where I sleep. The floor where Joanne is probably having her morning tea right now. All of it visible from here, above the canopy, standing in the middle of a jungle I almost did not enter.

"I left home, walked into the jungle, and from the top of the observation tower — I could see my own block. Twenty-third floor, right side. Some mornings hand you something you did not go looking for."

The Bike Park — A Fork, a Map, and a New World

I kept walking and came to a junction. A weathered wooden post stood at the fork, its painted arrows almost completely worn away by years of jungle humidity. Beside it, a large granite boulder, moss-covered and ancient. Two paths. I picked one and kept moving.

Around the next bend — a proper NParks information board. Chestnut Nature Park (Chestnut Bike Park). A full map in blue and pink, showing the complete network of biking trails looping through the park. Banked corners, switchbacks, the Pump Track circuit laid out clearly. On the right: Biking Etiquette rules. And in bold orange letters on the frame: "You Are Entering A Nature Park. Please Be Responsible For Your Own Safety."

After everything this morning — the blocked drain, the iron grate, the Protected Place gate, the live firing range — this map felt like a reward. No skulls. No warning signs in four languages. Just a proper park, built for people, completely open.

The Pump Track was right there on the map. I was close.

The Walk Home

I came down from the tower and walked back the way I had come — through the Bike Park, back along the Northern Loop trail, past the Bike Park map board, past the junction with the weathered wooden post, past the Northern Hiking Loop map board, and eventually back through the bollard entrance, along the perimeter fence, and out through the PUB gate to the road.

The morning had thrown four obstacles at me and I had handled each one correctly. Blocked drain — find another way. Iron grate — look and move on. Protected Place gate — read the sign, message David, step back. Live Firing Area — turn around immediately, no argument. Compass confusion — trust your own instinct over the AI's first answer.

Five thousand steps. Three and a half kilometres. One observation tower. And from the top of it — my own home, visible above the canopy.

That is Day 3.

What Three Days Taught Me

Day 1 at Chestnut was a clean loop. Beautiful, peaceful, 8,500 steps. Day 2 was an adventure — secret fence entry, cat and mouse with a recovery car worker, reservoir, getting briefly lost. Day 3 was something different again: an exploration with four obstacles, a live firing range, a compass that confused both me and my AI, and an observation tower I found by accident at the end of it all.

Three mornings. Three completely different mornings. The same forest — and each time it gave me something new.

Day 1 showed me the park. Day 2 showed me the secret. Day 3 showed me the limits — and then, just when I thought the morning was done, it handed me the best thing of all: standing above the treetops, seeing my own block, knowing exactly where home was.

Some mornings you plan. Some mornings find you. The best ones do both.

"I am 70 years old. I heard live gunfire in a Singapore jungle this morning, read a skull-and-crossbones sign in four languages, corrected my AI's navigation mistake, climbed an observation tower I never knew existed — and from the top of it, I saw my own home. Twenty-third floor. Right side. If that is not retirement done right, I do not know what is."
