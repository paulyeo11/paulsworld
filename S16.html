Why Alaska? Why Now?

There Is a Road Most People Will Never Drive有一条路，大多数人永远不会开车走

A Proposal  ·  Thirty Days  ·  The Last Frontier

I have been to Africa, to the Himalayas, to the bottom of the world in Antarctica. I have crossed deserts and dunes. But Alaska — wild, enormous, untamed Alaska — has been calling me for a very long time. And somewhere deep in that wilderness runs a road that most travellers never dare to take: the Dalton Highway, 414 miles of gravel, mud and ice that carves north from Fairbanks all the way to the Arctic Ocean.

The moment I watched the video about this road — people calling it America's most feared highway, a haul route built to serve the Trans-Alaska Pipeline, a place where your nearest mechanic is a hundred miles away and the bears do not care what make of car you drive — I knew. I have to go. Thirty days, the whole of Alaska, and that road.

This is my plan. I am writing it down so that when I finally stand at the edge of the Arctic Ocean with my boots in the gravel, I will remember the moment it all started — with an old man reading about a road and thinking: yes. That one.

"There is a road in Alaska that most people will never drive. I intend to drive it — all 414 miles, gravel and all, from Fairbanks to the top of the world."

The complete itinerary — from sea level to the Arctic

Alaska is the largest state in the United States — two and a half times the size of Texas. Thirty days sounds like a long time until you open the map. I have planned this trip in five regions, each one wilder than the last, ending with the great prize: the Dalton Highway and the Arctic Ocean.

Days 1–9 · The South

Anchorage, Kenai & Prince William Sound

The gentle beginning — before the wild road north

Every great journey needs a warm-up, and Alaska's south gives you one that would be the highlight of any other trip. I plan to spend the first three days in Anchorage — exploring the Tony Knowles Coastal Trail along Cook Inlet, the Alaska Native Heritage Center, and the Chugach Mountains rising behind the city like a wall. It looks a great deal like Queenstown, New Zealand: a modern city pressed against something vast and ancient.

Anchorage backed by the Chugach Mountains — a modern city pressed against something ancient and enormous.

Then south to the Kenai Peninsula — four days in what Alaskans call their own backyard playground. In Seward I plan to take the Kenai Fjords glacier cruise, which promises orcas surfacing between floating ice, puffins nesting in the cliffs, and tidewater glaciers calving into the sea with a sound like cannon fire. After that, the long drive to Homer, out on its famous Spit — a narrow strip of land jutting four miles into Kachemak Bay. I have read that Homer calls itself "the end of the road," and I love a place that knows exactly what it is.

Kenai Fjords National Park — a tidewater glacier calving into the sea. The crack and boom of it carries for miles.

Days 8 and 9 take me to Valdez for a cruise past the Columbia Glacier — one of the largest tidewater glaciers in North America — and a visit to the southern terminus of the Trans-Alaska Pipeline. I want to see where all that oil, which I will follow north for hundreds of miles, first meets the sea.

✦ ✦ ✦
Days 10–15 · The Interior

Denali & Fairbanks — Gateway to the Arctic

America's highest peak, then the Golden Heart City

Four days in Denali National Park — and I say four because one day is not enough. The park's private vehicles are restricted: you board a wildlife bus that carries you deep into the wilderness, and if you want to see Denali — the Great One, the highest peak in North America at 6,190 metres — you need time. The mountain hides behind cloud more often than not, and the old hands say you earn the view.

I want to do the Savage River hike on my first morning, getting my legs under me. Then a long bus tour to Wonder Lake, where on a clear day the reflection of Denali sits in the water like something from a dream. Bears, caribou, Dall sheep, moose — on a good day, all of them visible from the bus window. No fences. No zoo. Just the park, the wild, and whatever happens to be out that morning.

Wonder Lake, Denali — on a clear day the mountain reflects perfectly in the water. You earn this view.

Then to Fairbanks — Alaska's second city, called the Golden Heart City. Two days here before the big drive: I'll visit the Gold Dredge 8 and try my hand at gold panning (an old man with a pan in a creek — I expect I will love it), stand at the viewpoint above the Trans-Alaska Pipeline, and spend an evening at Chena Hot Springs, soaking under the midnight sun. In summer, the sun at Fairbanks barely sets — it dips toward the horizon around midnight and then climbs back up again. I intend to stay awake for it.

Fairbanks at midnight — the sun still above the horizon, the sky an impossible warm gold. You forget what night means.

"In Fairbanks at midnight the sun is still up. You sit outside in the warmth and wonder if you have somehow slipped into a different world — and in a way, you have."

Days 16–22 · The Heart of the Journey ★

The Dalton Highway — 414 Miles to the Top of the World

America's most feared road  ·  Fairbanks to the Arctic Ocean

This is the reason for the whole trip. The Dalton Highway — also called the Haul Road — was built in the 1970s to service the Trans-Alaska Pipeline during construction. It runs 414 miles from its junction near Fairbanks all the way north to Deadhorse and the Arctic Ocean. Most of it is gravel. There are only three places to get fuel along the entire route. Cell service: essentially none. Bears along the road: yes, frequently.

Most rental car companies will not allow you on it. Tour operators run guided vehicles, which is what I intend to use — a reputable guided expedition van, so I can look at the scenery without worrying about the tyres. But the adventure is entirely real.

The Dalton Highway heading north — 414 miles of gravel, tundra and pipeline, with almost nothing in between.
MILE 56

Day 16 — The Yukon River Crossing. The first great milestone: the highway crosses the Yukon River on a single-lane bridge. There is a small camp here — hot food, fuel, and the sight of that great brown river sliding slowly westward to the sea. I will stop, eat something warm, and feel the road beginning in earnest.

MILE 115

Day 17 — Crossing the Arctic Circle. At mile 115, a large wooden sign marks the Arctic Circle — latitude 66°33' North, the line above which the midnight sun never sets in summer. I have crossed the equator. I have been to Antarctica. But the Arctic Circle on foot, in Alaska, is something I have not yet done. There will be a photograph. There will be a certificate. There will be a seventy-year-old man grinning like a schoolboy.

The Arctic Circle sign at Mile 115, Dalton Highway — 66°33' North. The midnight sun does not set beyond this line.
MILE 175

Days 18–19 — Coldfoot & Wiseman. Coldfoot Camp is the last real service stop before Deadhorse — 238 miles to the north with nothing in between. Two nights here, using it as a base. From Coldfoot I'll make the short drive to Wiseman, an old gold mining village of barely two dozen residents, which has been sitting in the same spot since 1910. It is one of those places that time has simply walked around. I also plan a day hike into Gates of the Arctic National Park — one of the most remote national parks in the United States, where there are no roads, no trails, and no rangers at the trailhead telling you what to do.

MILE 244

Day 20 — Atigun Pass and the Brooks Range. The road climbs to Atigun Pass — 4,800 feet, the highest highway mountain pass in Alaska — crossing the spine of the Brooks Range. On one side: boreal forest and river valleys. On the other: the open Arctic tundra of the North Slope, stretching flat and treeless all the way to the sea. It is one of those moments on a road trip where you come over a crest and everything changes.

Atigun Pass, 4,800 ft — crossing the Brooks Range. North of here: the Arctic tundra, flat to the horizon.
MILE 414

Day 21 — Deadhorse and the End of the Road. Deadhorse is not a tourist destination. It is an oil town — a working, industrial settlement on the Arctic Coastal Plain where the workers wear hard hats and the streets are gravel and the only reason anyone lives here is the oil beneath the permafrost. And yet arriving here after 414 miles of wilderness road feels like something enormous. The Trans-Alaska Pipeline — that silver thread I first saw at Fairbanks and then followed north for days — ends here. Road's end. The top of America.

Deadhorse, Prudhoe Bay — Mile 414, the end of the road. The pipeline ends here too, 800 miles from where it started.

Day 22 — The Arctic Ocean. You cannot simply walk to the Arctic Ocean from Deadhorse — the oil fields are private property and require a guided security tour. I have already booked one. The tour takes you through the gates, past the drilling rigs and storage tanks, and out to the actual beach on the Arctic Ocean. I intend to crouch down and put my hand in that water. I do not care how cold it is. That water will have touched the ice floes of the High Arctic, the polar bears, the narwhals. I want to touch it back.

The Arctic Ocean at Prudhoe Bay — flat, grey, and immense. I will put my hand in this water.

"When I finally crouch at the edge of the Arctic Ocean and put my hand in that water — that cold, ancient, polar water — I think I will understand something that no map can teach you."

That afternoon: fly Deadhorse to Fairbanks — a short flight that would take two days to drive back. The road has given me everything. Now I am ready to rest.

✦ ✦ ✦
Days 23–30 · The Grand Finale

Hot Springs, Glaciers & Southeast Alaska

Resting, then one last sweep of magnificence

After the Dalton, Days 23 and 24 are for recovery. Chena Hot Springs, northeast of Fairbanks — natural geothermal pools in the wilderness, where I plan to soak every aching muscle and eat something that is not trail mix. Then the most spectacular flight I can imagine: across to Juneau, Alaska's capital city, which can only be reached by air or sea (there are no roads to Juneau — Alaska is still that wild).

Chena Hot Springs — soaking tired bones in geothermal water surrounded by the Alaska wilderness. Earned, every degree of it.

Three days in Juneau: walking to the Mendenhall Glacier (a glacier you can walk up to from a car park — an extraordinary and slightly surreal thing), whale watching in the Gastineau Channel where humpbacks surface in plain view from the ferry dock, and a seaplane flight over the icefields — because at some point you must simply get above it all and look down.

Days 28 and 29 bring the final masterpiece: a full-day cruise through Glacier Bay National Park. Sixteen tidewater glaciers flowing into the sea. Humpback whales. Sea otters floating on their backs. Brown bears on the shore. The boat moves slowly through the ice-cold water and the glaciers groan and calve around you. I have been told it is the most spectacular single day's scenery in all of Alaska. That is a big claim in a state of this size. I intend to find out.

Glacier Bay National Park — a tidewater glacier calves into the sea. The sound arrives a second after the ice falls.

Day 30: fly back to Anchorage, then home. Thirty days in the wildest state in America, from the sea-level forests of the south to the gravel road at the top of the world.

Before I Go

What I Am Planning to Pack & Know

Practical notes for the road

Alaska in summer (June–August) is the recommended window — midnight sun, wildlife most active, roads open. The Dalton can be impassable in winter. Temperatures on the North Slope in summer are surprisingly mild, 10–18°C, but weather changes fast. I'll layer up and bring waterproofs.

My Pre-Trip Checklist

  1. Book the Dalton Highway guided tour early. Reputable operators fill up fast in summer. A good guided van removes the rental-car restriction problem and gives you an expert who knows where the bears are.
  2. Book the Prudhoe Bay Arctic Ocean tour separately. You cannot walk to the Arctic Ocean — it requires a security-cleared tour. Book in advance through a Deadhorse operator.
  3. Fly Deadhorse to Fairbanks, don't drive back. The road takes two days each way. Flying (around 40 minutes) saves four days of repeating terrain you have already seen and costs around $300–400.
  4. Glacier Bay: book the day cruise early. Day cruise permits into Glacier Bay are limited. Book months ahead in peak season.
  5. Bear spray. Always. Brown bears on the Dalton. Grizzlies in Denali. Black bears on the Kenai. Alaska means bear country. Carry spray, know how to use it.
  6. Travel insurance with medical evacuation. Remote Alaska is remote. The nearest hospital from Coldfoot is over 200 miles. Full coverage including air evacuation is non-negotiable.
  7. Satellite communicator (e.g. Garmin inReach). No cell service on the Dalton. A satellite messaging device is a small, sensible insurance policy.

Best time to go: mid-June to mid-July — peak midnight sun, Denali's roads open, Glacier Bay accessible, the Dalton muddy but passable. Mosquitoes are real and enthusiastic from late June onward. I will pack a head net and make my peace with them.

Why This Trip

The Road Is Still Out There Waiting

A note to myself — and to you

I have been writing about travel for a while now — Africa, Nepal, Patagonia, Antarctica. People sometimes ask me: what is left? The honest answer is: a great deal. The world is very large and I am only one man with one pair of legs. But Alaska has always stood apart in my imagination. Not just for the scenery — though the scenery is extraordinary — but for what the place represents. A frontier. A genuine last edge of something. A road that tests you.

The Dalton Highway is 414 miles of gravel between you and the top of the continent. No shortcuts. No cell service. Bears in the road and tundra to every horizon. That is not a warning. That is the reason.

"I am seventy years old. I retired early so I could see things. Alaska has been on the list a long time. The road is still out there. So am I."

What I Hope Alaska Will Teach Me

  1. Scale. The world is far larger than our daily life suggests. Alaska — just one state — is two and a half times the size of Texas. Standing in it will make me very small and very grateful.
  2. Silence. The Dalton Highway offers a kind of quiet that cities cannot give — hundreds of miles with no crowd, no traffic, no notifications. Just road and sky and the sound of gravel under the wheels.
  3. That the hard roads lead somewhere good. The Dalton is difficult by design. But at the end of it is the Arctic Ocean. The hard way is usually the right way.
  4. To keep going. At seventy, the greatest danger is not the road. It is the chair. Alaska is my answer to the chair.

I will write the full story when I come home — road-stained, sore, and full of it. For now, this is the plan. Alaska, here I come. 🐻❄🛣
