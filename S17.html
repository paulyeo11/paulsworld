Teaching Jeremy — How I Used AI to Help My Friend Get Organised

June 2026  ·  Paul Yeo

My friend Jeremy is 70 years old — just like me. But unlike me, he is completely lost when it comes to computers. Yet he runs a property portfolio, manages multiple businesses, and somehow keeps everything in his head. I decided to change that.

Jeremy and I go back a long way. He is one of those people who is brilliantly sharp in business — sharp instincts, sharp deals — but absolutely hopeless when it comes to organising himself. His desk looks like a small earthquake hit it. His files are everywhere. He tracks his properties on scraps of paper, in WhatsApp groups, and somewhere inside his head.

One day I asked him, "Jeremy, how do you even know what you own?" He laughed and said, "I just know lah." That is a very Malaysian answer. But I was not satisfied.

The Idea That Changed Everything改变一切的想法

I have been using Claude — an AI assistant — for almost a year now. I use it to build my website, manage my investments, plan my travels, even write these stories. And slowly, an idea began forming in my mind: what if I could teach Jeremy to use AI, not just chat with it, but actually use it to solve his real problems?

Not Claude Chat — anyone can do that. I wanted to teach him Claude Code. A tool where you can ask AI to build things for you — reports, databases, websites, trackers. All by describing what you want in plain English.

The Mission Jeremy is in Malaysia. I am in Indonesia. Starting next week, I will teach him remotely using AnyDesk — where I can see and control his screen from across the border. One session per week. Patient, practical, no jargon.

Why Jeremy Needs This为什么杰里米需要这个

Let me paint the picture clearly. Jeremy has multiple properties spread across Malaysia. Each one has different tenants, different rental rates, different maintenance needs, different renewal dates. He is trying to manage all of this from memory and loose files.

On top of that, he runs a business with an ERP system — a database that tracks sales, inventory, and operations. But he does not know how to extract the reports he actually needs from it. So he either guesses, or he asks someone else, or he goes without.

This is not a technology problem. This is an organisation problem that technology can solve — if someone just shows him how.

The Teaching Plan教学计划

Here is what I have designed for Jeremy, step by step over the coming weeks:

1

Week 1–2: Get Comfortable with the Computer

Install VS Code, open a terminal, learn to type simple commands. No pressure. Just comfort.

2

Week 3–4: First Magic with Claude Code

Install Claude Code together. Ask it to build a simple webpage. Watch it work. Feel the excitement.

3

Week 5–6: Property Tracker

Build a simple property management dashboard — all his units, tenants, rental dates in one clean view.

4

Week 7–8: ERP Reports

Export his ERP data as CSV, let Claude Code read it, and generate the custom reports he actually needs.

What I Have Learned About Teaching

Teaching someone older than sixty requires a completely different mindset. Speed kills. Jargon kills. Impatience kills. You have to slow down, celebrate every small win, and never make them feel stupid.

The secret is this: connect every new skill to their real problem. Not "learn how to use a terminal." But "let us open this box so you can see your rental income." The motivation has to be personal and immediate.

You do not teach an old dog new tricks by showing him tricks. You show him the reward first — then the trick becomes the easy part.

I know this because I was once that old dog too. When I first started building my website on an iPhone at age 69, people thought I was mad. No computer science background. No coding experience. Just curiosity and stubbornness. And Claude as my patient teacher.

Now I am passing that gift forward to Jeremy. If I could learn this at 69, he can learn this at 70. I genuinely believe that.

The Takeaway

Age is not the barrier. Confidence is. When you show an older person that the tool actually works — that it does what they asked, that it solves their real problem — something lights up in them. The fear melts away.

Jeremy does not need to become a programmer. He just needs to become someone who knows how to describe what he wants — and let AI do the rest. That is a skill anyone can learn. Even at 70.

Next week, we begin. I cannot wait to see his face when Claude Code builds his first property dashboard and he realises — for the very first time — that his chaos can be tamed.

— Paul Yeo, June 2026
