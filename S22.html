Born at Kandang Kerbau
My Singapore Roots

By Paul Yeo  ·  26 December 1956  ·  Singapore

Every life has a beginning. Mine started on a cool December night at one of Singapore's most beloved hospitals — a place where generations of Singaporeans drew their first breath.

I was born on the 26th of December 1956, just past nine-thirty in the evening, at Kandang Kerbau Maternity Hospital in Singapore. KK Hospital, as everyone calls it, was the birthplace of countless Singaporeans during those post-war years when the island was still finding its feet. For my family, it was the place where a new chapter began.

A Hokkien Family一个福建家庭

My roots are Hokkien — a dialect group that traces its origins to the Fujian province of southern China. My father came from China, a merchant who made his life in Singapore. He spoke Hokkien, carried Chinese nationality, and built a home at Beach Road. My mother, who came from across the Dutch East Indies, was also Hokkien by heritage — the dialect that bound so many overseas Chinese families across Southeast Asia in those days.

Growing up, Hokkien was the language of the kitchen, the language of family gatherings, the language of home. It carried the texture of my parents' stories — of migration, of struggle, of starting over in a new land.

The World I Was Born Into我出生时的世界

Singapore in 1956 was a British Crown Colony, on the cusp of great change. The Federation of Malaya was still a year away. Lee Kuan Yew's People's Action Party had just been founded the year before. The island was buzzing with the energy of a people beginning to imagine their own future.

My parents could not have known what Singapore would become — that within a generation, this small island would transform into one of the most prosperous nations on earth. But they were here, putting down roots, and on that December night, they brought me into the world.

"To know where you are going,
you must first know where you came from."

Retrieving the Record找回那份记录

Sixty-nine years later, I retrieved my official birth certificate from ICA — a crisp digital extract, clean and clear, a document that confirms what I have always known: I am a Singaporean, born and bred, with Hokkien blood and a story worth telling.

Holding that document — and the yellowed 1969 certified copy my family kept all these years — is a strange and moving experience. It is proof that I existed before I have any memory of existing. It connects me to my parents, to their journey, to the Singapore that was.

A Legacy to Pass On

I think about my children and grandchildren sometimes. These documents, these records, are not just bureaucratic paper. They are the thread that connects them to their great-grandparents, to China, to the Dutch East Indies, to a merchant on Beach Road who dared to build a life in a new country.

That is the real value of a birth certificate. Not the paper itself — but the story it represents. A family that crossed oceans, spoke Hokkien around the dinner table, and on one December night in 1956, welcomed a new life into the world.

That life is mine. And I am grateful for every year of it.
