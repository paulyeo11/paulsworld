Chapter One · S1

Lost in MacRitchie — The Day Police Rescued Me From the Forest

🌿 Singapore  ·  April 2023

On April 10, 2023, my friend and I got completely lost inside MacRitchie Nature Trail and Reservoir Park. What followed was a rescue operation involving the Singapore Police Force, the Gurkha Contingent, the Aerial Response Team, and officers from NParks and PUB. We made the national news.

It started simply enough. My friend, aged 51, and I decided to go for a hike. We left my house at 8am feeling confident and well-prepared — or so we thought. MacRitchie is a well-known nature reserve in the heart of Singapore. Families go there. School children go there. It did not seem dangerous.

We did not bring enough water. We did not have offline maps. We did not tell anyone exactly which trail we were taking. Three classic mistakes — and we made all three before 9am.

At 3pm, we reached the Ruins of Syonan Jinja — the old Japanese shrine deep inside the reserve. It was an interesting landmark — but we had a serious problem. We could not find the trail back out. Every path looked the same. The jungle was dense, the light was fading, and my phone battery was at 12%.

We tried three different paths. Each time, we ended up back at the same spot. We were going in circles. That was the moment I admitted to myself: we are genuinely lost.

Then it started to rain heavily. We were soaked, exhausted, and completely off the trail. My friend's battery was dead. Mine was barely alive. We called 999.

Chapter Three · S3

The Day I Lost My iPhone in Portugal

📱 Portugal  ·  June 2025

How did a spare phone, a local SIM card, and two helpful Portuguese police officers turn a disaster into a story worth telling?

On 23 June 2025, I was travelling solo in Portugal. At seventy, I still prefer hostels and public buses to package tours — they keep the trip honest and the wallet light.

That afternoon, I boarded a Rede Expressos bus in Albufeira. Somewhere along the way, the bus pulled into a roadside stop for a short break. I rushed to the toilet, then bought myself an ice cream from a small stall nearby. I sat down in the shade and enjoyed it — the kind of small, simple pleasure that makes solo travel feel good at seventy.

When the driver called everyone back, I stood up in a hurry, finished the last bite, and climbed onto the bus. I did not look back at the table.

The bus had already pulled onto the highway when my hand reached into my pocket and found nothing. I checked the other pocket. My bag. The seat beside me. Nothing.

I felt my chest tighten. This was my new iPhone. All my travel photos, my booking confirmations, my contacts, my maps — everything I needed to keep travelling was in that phone. And we were already on the highway, moving fast.

At seventy, you learn that panic does not help anything. But knowing that does not always stop the panic from coming.

Sitting on that little boat along the Algarve coast the next day — the yellow sandstone cliffs, the sea arches, the sea caves — I thought to myself: I am seventy years old, alone in a foreign country, and I solved this myself. That feeling was worth more than the phone.

About the Author

Paul Yeo retired at 67 and has not slowed down since. A member of Toastmasters, an adventurer, and now a first-time author, he believes that retirement is not an ending — it is the beginning of the most creative chapter of your life.

My Travel is his first book, compiled from real stories published at paulsworld.vercel.app.
