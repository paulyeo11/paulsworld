6 unmissable experiences — these are the highlights that make Finland & the Baltic States unforgettable. The icebreaker cruise and the van tour are the two absolute must-dos.
1
🧊 Icebreaker Cruise — Gulf of Bothnia
Day 5–6 · Kemi, Finland · THE highlight
▼
Walk on the Frozen Sea
A real working icebreaker ship smashes through the frozen Gulf of Bothnia between Finland and Sweden. The highlight: you step OFF the ship onto the ice and walk on a frozen sea. The ice in January–February is thick enough to stand on safely. Nothing else feels like this.
Book the MORNING cruise — the experience is best in full daylight. Go in January–February for the thickest ice. The original Polar Explorer cruise (smaller, more intimate) may be discontinued — check for Sampo or current operators out of Kemi.
⭐ #1 Experience of the Entire Trip
The Lunch at the Fishing Village
After the cruise, lunch is at a restaurant in a fishing village on the Swedish border. Fresh fish, local bread, a warm interior after the frozen sea. One of those meals you remember for years.
2
🚐 Small Group Van Tour — Riga to Tallinn
Day 19–20 · Latvia → Estonia · Hidden gems
▼
Two Countries, One Day, Maximum 8 People
A private van tour (max 8 persons) connecting Riga and Tallinn via places impossible to reach by public transport — national parks, medieval ruins, UNESCO heritage sites hidden in the Estonian and Latvian countryside. This route can run either direction (Riga→Tallinn or Tallinn→Riga) depending on your itinerary.
Book well in advance — small group tours fill up fast. The small group size means the guide can take you off the beaten path. This is not a coach tour.
⭐ Best Way to See Between the Capitals
3
🎅 Santa Claus Village — Rovaniemi
Day 4–5 · Arctic Circle, Finland
▼
On the Arctic Circle
The official Santa Claus Village sits exactly on the Arctic Circle in Rovaniemi. In January–February, it's a snow-covered winter wonderland — reindeer rides, husky safaris, Northern Lights, and the best salmon you will eat on the entire trip.
Eat the salmon here. It is the best salmon on the trip — do not miss it.
🐟 Must Eat: Salmon at Santa Claus Village
4
🏰 Tallinn Medieval Old Town
Day 21–25 · Estonia
▼
Best-Preserved Medieval City in Europe
Tallinn's old town is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the most intact medieval cities in all of Europe. Cobblestone streets, fortified towers, the Town Hall Square, and views from Toompea Hill over the red rooftops. In winter, you have it almost entirely to yourself.
Stay in the old town itself — walking distance to everything. Evening walks on the illuminated medieval streets are magical in winter.
5
🎨 Riga — Little Paris of the Baltics
Day 15–18 · Latvia
▼
Art Nouveau Capital of the World
Riga has more Art Nouveau buildings than any other city on earth — ornate facades, elaborate ironwork, sculpted faces above every door. The old town is a UNESCO site. The Central Market (in converted Zeppelin hangars) is one of Europe's largest. Always called the "Little Paris" of the Baltic States.
Walk along Alberta Street for the best concentration of Art Nouveau facades. The old town and the market are must-visits.
6
⛪ Vilnius — Baroque Old Town
Day 10–13 · Lithuania
▼
Eastern Europe's Hidden Baroque Gem
Vilnius has one of the largest surviving baroque old towns in Europe — over 1,500 historic buildings, hidden courtyards, and the famous Gates of Dawn. Very affordable, low on tourists in winter, and full of character. The microstate of Užupis (Republic of Užupis) inside the city is a quirky bonus.
Most affordable capital of the three Baltic states. Great local food and coffee culture. Explore the hidden courtyards — they are everywhere.
Finland and the Baltic states are serious food destinations. Here are the must-eats — from a frozen sea to a Finnish café.
🐟
Fresh Salmon
⭐ MUST EAT · Santa Claus Village, Rovaniemi
The best salmon on the entire trip. The restaurant at Santa Claus Village on the Arctic Circle serves salmon that is fresh, perfectly prepared, and unlike anything you find in Singapore. Do not leave Rovaniemi without eating this.
⭐ David's #1 Must Eat
🍽️
Lunch at the Fishing Village
Swedish border · Icebreaker cruise day
After stepping off the icebreaker and walking on the frozen sea, lunch is at a local restaurant in a fishing village right on the border with Sweden. Fresh fish, local bread, a warm room after the cold. Included in the icebreaker cruise day programme.
🥐
Korvapuusti (Finnish Cinnamon Roll)
Any café in Helsinki or Rovaniemi
The Finnish cinnamon roll is bigger, chewier, and more cardamom-forward than anything called a "cinnamon roll" elsewhere. It is a legal requirement to eat at least one in Finland. Every café has them. They are best warm, with strong coffee.
🥩
Finnish Steak
Helsinki restaurant
Scandinavian beef is excellent — well-raised, well-rested, well-cooked. Helsinki has a strong restaurant scene. Worth a proper sit-down dinner on your Helsinki days.
🍺
Baltic Rye Bread & Local Beer
Vilnius · Riga · Tallinn
Each Baltic capital has its own version of dense dark rye bread — heavy, slightly sour, unforgettable with butter and smoked fish. Pair with a local craft beer in any old town pub. Very affordable in all three capitals.
☕
Finnish Coffee Culture
Throughout Finland
Finland is the world's #1 coffee-consuming country per capita. The coffee is strong, the cafés are warm, and sitting with a coffee and a cinnamon roll while snow falls outside is one of the great simple pleasures of the trip.
🧊Icebreaker Cruise — Critical Tips▼
  • Book the MORNING cruise — the daylight experience is far better than the afternoon. Do not book the afternoon slot.
  • Go in January–February for the thickest ice. The sea needs to be properly frozen to walk on it. December is too early — ice may not be thick enough.
  • The original Polar Explorer cruise (smaller, more intimate) may be discontinued or renamed. Check for Sampo icebreaker or current operators in Kemi. Sampo is larger and goes further out — more expensive but worth it.
  • Transport: Coach picks up at Rovaniemi (~3 hrs to Kemi/Gulf of Bothnia). Do not arrange your own transport.
  • Dress in layers: thermal base layer, insulating mid-layer, windproof outer layer. Waterproof boots essential. The ship provides flotation suits for walking on the ice.
  • Walking on the ice is the highlight. Do not skip this part of the experience.
🚂Night Train Helsinki → Rovaniemi▼
  • Book a sleeper cabin — the night train is an experience in itself. Private cabin, bed, breakfast delivered. You travel 800 km overnight and wake up in Rovaniemi.
  • Operated by VR (Finnish Railways). Book online well in advance, especially in January–February (peak Lapland season).
  • Departs Helsinki around 17:00–18:00, arrives Rovaniemi ~08:00 next morning.
  • This is far better than flying — you save a night's hotel, arrive rested, and the journey itself is memorable.
🚐Riga–Tallinn Van Tour▼
  • Maximum 8 persons — this is a small group private tour, not a coach. Book well in advance.
  • Can run Riga→Tallinn or Tallinn→Riga depending on your itinerary direction.
  • Covers national parks, medieval ruins, and UNESCO sites that are impossible to reach by public transport or individual travel.
  • The guide takes you off the tourist trail — this is the hidden side of the Baltic states.
  • Search for "Riga to Tallinn private tour" or "Baltic road trip small group" — several operators run this route.
✈️Getting Between Cities▼
  • Helsinki → Vilnius: Fly (due to time constraints — coach is possible but long). Budget airlines like Finnair, Ryanair, Wizz Air cover this route.
  • Vilnius → Riga: Coach, ~4 hours. Very affordable. Lux Express is the best operator.
  • Riga → Tallinn: Via van tour, or Lux Express coach (~4.5 hours).
  • Tallinn → Helsinki: Ferry, ~2 hours. Tallink Silja or Viking Line. A scenic end to the Baltic leg.
  • The ferry from Tallinn to Helsinki across the Gulf of Finland is a beautiful crossing — book a cabin if travelling overnight.
🌡️Weather & What to Pack▼
  • Finland (Jan–Feb): −10°C to −20°C in Rovaniemi. Not as cold as it sounds if you're dressed properly.
  • Baltic states (Jan–Feb): Milder, −2°C to +5°C. Occasional rain, especially in Lithuania.
  • December (if you visit then): Milder than January, some rain in the Baltics. Not cold enough for guaranteed thick icebreaker ice.
  • Essential: thermal base layers, insulating mid-layer, windproof waterproof outer jacket, waterproof boots with good grip, wool socks, hat, gloves, scarf.
  • The icebreaker provides flotation suits — but you dress in your own thermals underneath.
💳Money & Practical Info▼
  • Currency: Euro (€) in all four countries — Finland, Lithuania, Latvia, Estonia all use EUR. No currency exchange needed between countries.
  • Card payments accepted almost everywhere in Finland and Estonia. Lithuania and Latvia also largely cashless.
  • Finland and Estonia are the most expensive. Lithuania is the most affordable of the three Baltic states.
  • Visa: Singapore passport holders do not need a visa for Schengen (Finland) or the Baltic states (all Schengen). 90-day limit.
  • SIM card: Buy a Finnish SIM at Helsinki airport (works across all Schengen countries). Or use Airalo eSIM before you fly.
⭐Savonlinna — Optional Gem▼
  • Savonlinna is a medieval castle town on an island in Lake Saimaa, ~330 km northeast of Helsinki. Not many people know about it.
  • Reached by train from Helsinki (~3.5 hrs) + lake ferry. A day trip is tight but possible; an overnight stay is better.
  • The centrepiece is Olavinlinna Castle — a 15th-century fortress on its own island in the middle of the lake. One of the best-preserved medieval castles in Northern Europe.
  • Famous for the Savonlinna Opera Festival (July) — outdoor opera performed inside the castle walls. In winter it is quiet and atmospheric.
  • Best added as a separate stop between Helsinki and Rovaniemi, rather than as a day trip from Helsinki.
🌌Northern Lights in Rovaniemi▼
  • Rovaniemi in January–February is one of the best places in the world to see the Northern Lights (Aurora Borealis).
  • You need: clear skies, darkness (after ~5pm), and solar activity. January–February has the longest dark periods.
  • Best viewed away from city lights — any husky safari or reindeer tour will take you to a dark spot.
  • Download the Aurora Forecast app (Space Weather) before you go. Check it every evening.
  • Even if clouds don't cooperate, the snow-covered Lapland landscape at night is beautiful on its own.
